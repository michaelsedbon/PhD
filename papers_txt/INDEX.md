## Experimental evolution and the dynamics of adaptation and genome evolution in microbial populations
**File:** `Experimental_evolution_and_the_dynamics.txt`
**Subjects:** ST Ctrl DE
**URL:** https://www.nature.com/articles/ismej201769?utm_source=chatgpt.com

This review covers the Long-Term Evolution Experiment (LTEE) with *E. coli*, spanning over 60,000 generations. It examines how microbial populations adapt through beneficial mutations, selective sweeps, and clonal interference. Key findings include decelerating but ongoing fitness gains, the evolution of hypermutability via defects in DNA repair, and ecological diversification (e.g., citrate utilization in one lineage). The paper discusses the interplay between genome evolution and phenotypic adaptation, highlighting that many mutations are passengers rather than drivers of adaptation. It also reviews how genomic data reveal complex dynamics including epistasis, historical contingency, and cryptic genetic variation.

## An orthogonal T7 replisome for continuous hypermutation and accelerated evolution in E. coli

**Subjects:** HyperMutation Systems
**URL:** https://www.science.org/doi/abs/10.1126/science.adp9583?utm_source=chatgpt.com

This paper introduces T7-ORACLE, an orthogonal T7 replisome-based system in *E. coli* for continuous in vivo hypermutation and accelerated directed evolution. The authors engineer T7 DNA polymerase variants to achieve mutation rates ~100,000-fold above background, specifically on circular plasmid templates replicated by the orthogonal replisome. As a proof of concept, the authors evolve TEM-1 β-lactamase for enhanced activity against cefotaxime, achieving a substantial increase in resistance within days. T7-ORACLE represents a major step toward scalable, hands-free continuous evolution.

## Adaptive prediction of environmental changes by microorganisms
**File:** `Adaptive_prediction_of_environmental_changes.txt`
**Subjects:** ST Ctrl DE
**URL:** https://longitude.weizmann.ac.il/sites/longitude_pilpel/files/publications/2009_Mitchell.pdf

This study demonstrates that microorganisms can anticipate environmental changes, drawing a parallel to Pavlovian conditioning. In *E. coli* and *S. cerevisiae*, exposure to a first environmental stimulus pre-activates genes needed for a subsequent, predictable stimulus. For example, lactose induces genes for maltose utilization in *E. coli*, reflecting the natural order of sugar encounter in the mammalian gut. This anticipatory regulation improves fitness in the second environment. Strains evolved under reversed stimulus order lose this conditioned response, confirming it is an adaptive, evolved trait. The work reveals how temporal regularities in natural environments are encoded in gene regulatory networks.

## Cell-autonomous adaptation: an overlooked avenue of adaptation in human evolution
**File:** `Cell-autonomous_adaptation__an_overlooked_avenue.txt`
**Subjects:** N/A
**URL:** https://www.cell.com/trends/genetics/fulltext/S0168-9525(24)00260-9

This perspective introduces cell-autonomous adaptations (CAAs) — inherited traits that enhance individual cell function and thereby boost organismal fitness. Unlike adaptations requiring multicellular organization (e.g., organ-level changes), CAAs can manifest in isolated cells. Examples include adaptations to hypoxia and cold in high-altitude and Arctic populations. The authors show that CAAs parallel adaptations in unicellular organisms, and by analyzing selection signals in Andean highlanders, they identify novel candidate CAAs. The concept bridges unicellular and multicellular evolution, suggesting that many important adaptations in complex organisms originate at the cellular level and can be studied experimentally in simpler systems.

## Peptide sequencing via reverse translation of peptides into DNA
**File:** `Peptide_sequencing_via_reverse_translation.txt`
**Subjects:** Reverse Translation
**URL:** https://www.biorxiv.org/content/10.1101/2024.05.31.596913v1

This paper presents a novel peptide sequencing method based on "reverse translation" — converting amino acid sequences into DNA barcodes. Through a modified Edman degradation cycle, amino acids are iteratively tagged with DNA oligonucleotides encoding their identity and position. A proximity extension assay (PEA) then concatenates these barcodes, which are read by standard DNA sequencing. The approach can identify amino acid identity, position within the peptide, and the originating peptide molecule. It detects single amino acid substitutions and maps post-translational modifications. The method is inherently scalable and parallel, offering a path toward high-throughput de novo protein sequencing with single-molecule sensitivity.

## Real-time dynamic single-molecule protein sequencing on an integrated semiconductor device
**File:** `Real-time_dynamic_single-molecule_protein_sequencing.txt`
**Subjects:** Reverse Translation
**URL:** https://www.science.org/doi/10.1126/science.abo7651

This paper describes a semiconductor chip-based platform for real-time single-molecule protein sequencing. Individual peptides are immobilized and probed by dye-labeled N-terminal amino acid recognizers, while aminopeptidases progressively cleave the peptide chain. The system identifies amino acids based on fluorescence intensity, fluorescence lifetime, and binding kinetics of the recognizers. It can detect post-translational modifications and distinguish peptide variants. The technology parallels next-generation DNA sequencing in its use of integrated semiconductor chips, aiming to provide a sensitive, scalable, and accessible tool for proteomics.

## Evolutionary dynamics on graphs
**File:** `Evolutionary_dynamics_on_graphs.txt`
**Subjects:** Directed evoltion concepts
**URL:** https://www.nature.com/articles/nature03204

This foundational paper generalizes evolutionary dynamics by placing populations on graph structures rather than assuming well-mixed populations. Each node represents an individual and edges define reproductive interactions. The authors derive fixation probabilities for mutants on various graph topologies and show that certain structures (e.g., star graphs, scale-free networks) act as "amplifiers of selection," increasing the fixation probability of advantageous mutants beyond the well-mixed prediction. Other topologies suppress selection. The framework applies to ecology, cancer biology, multicellular development, and social networks, providing a mathematical foundation for understanding how spatial structure influences evolutionary outcomes.

## The DarTG toxin-antitoxin system provides phage defence by ADP-ribosylating viral DNA
**File:** `The_DarTG_toxin-antitoxin_system_provides.txt`
**Subjects:** Directed evolution Moving Target
**URL:** https://www.nature.com/articles/s41564-022-01153-5

This study reveals that the DarTG toxin-antitoxin (TA) system functions as a phage defense mechanism. The DarT toxin is a DNA ADP-ribosyltransferase that modifies phage DNA upon infection, blocking viral replication and preventing progeny production. The DarG antitoxin reverses the modification to protect host DNA. The authors identify mechanisms by which phages evolve resistance, including mutations in their DNA polymerase or acquisition of anti-DarT factors. The work significantly expands the known functions of TA systems beyond their roles in plasmid maintenance and stress response, positioning them as a widespread innate immune strategy in bacteria.

## Germinal centers output clonally diverse plasma cell populations expressing high- and low-affinity antibodies
**File:** `Germinal_centers_output_clonally_diverse.txt`
**Subjects:** HyperMutation Systems, Germinal centers
**URL:** https://www.cell.com/cell/fulltext/S0092-8674%2823%2901173-X?utm_source=chatgpt.com

This paper demonstrates that germinal centers (GCs) in influenza infection and immunization do not exclusively select the highest-affinity B cell clones. Instead, GCs co-mature a clonally diverse population of B cells spanning a wide range of antibody affinities, and plasma cell differentiation is not strictly limited to high-affinity variants. This broad selection may represent an evolutionary compromise to produce diverse serum antibody responses capable of covering viral heterogeneity. Immunization-induced GCs are less productive overall but still support plasma cells with varied affinities. The findings challenge the classical view that GCs act as strict affinity filters.

## Orthogonal replication with optogenetic selection evolves yeast JEN1 into a mevalonate transporter
**File:** `Orthogonal_replication_with_optogenetic_selection.txt`
**Subjects:** HyperMutation Systems
**URL:** https://www.embopress.org/doi/full/10.1038/s44320-025-00113-5

This paper introduces OptoRep, a system combining continuous directed evolution (via OrthoRep) with optogenetic selection to fine-tune selection pressure. The authors engineer a mevalonate-auxotrophic yeast strain with light-controlled growth and evolve the yeast JEN1 monocarboxylate transporter into a mevalonate importer — a gain-of-function mutation. Light-tunable selection allowed gradual increases in stringency, facilitating the evolution of mutations that would be inaccessible under constant strong selection. The evolved transporter improved farnesene production. The work demonstrates how combining orthogonal mutagenesis with optogenetic selection enables the evolution of challenging gain-of-function phenotypes.

## Linking signaling and selection in the germinal center
**File:** `Linking_signaling_and_selection_in.txt`
**Subjects:** Germinal centers
**URL:** https://onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/epdf/10.1111/imr.12744

This review discusses the molecular mechanisms of B cell selection in germinal centers (GCs), focusing on how BCR signaling and T follicular helper (TFH) cell interactions coordinate affinity maturation. Positive selection in the light zone requires antigen capture and T cell help, with c-Myc upregulation serving as a marker of successfully selected cells. The paper explores the zonal organization of GCs, the role of antigen presentation by follicular dendritic cells, and the integration of multiple signaling pathways that determine B cell fate — recycling to the dark zone for further mutation, versus differentiation into memory B cells or plasma cells.

## Engineering an allosteric transcription factor to respond to new ligands
**File:** `Engineering_an_allosteric_transcription_factor.txt`
**Subjects:** HyperMutation Systems
**URL:** https://www.nature.com/articles/nmeth.3696

This paper presents a strategy for engineering allosteric transcription factors (aTFs) to sense new ligands. Using the *E. coli* LacI repressor as a model, the authors combine computational ligand docking, single-residue saturation mutagenesis of the ligand-binding pocket, and error-prone PCR to produce LacI variants responsive to fucose, gentiobiose, lactitol, and sucralose. Importantly, the newly engineered specificities retain proper allosteric regulation (i.e., ligand binding still induces the conformational change that derepresses transcription). The approach establishes a general framework for creating custom biosensors and regulatory switches for metabolic engineering and synthetic biology.

## Following evolutionary paths to protein-protein interactions with high affinity and selectivity
**File:** `Following_evolutionary_paths_to_protein-protein.txt`
**Subjects:** N/A
**URL:** https://www.nature.com/articles/nsmb.1670

This study traces the evolutionary paths by which new protein–protein interactions with high affinity and selectivity emerge, using the colicin–immunity protein (ColE–Im) system as a model. Starting from Im9, which weakly inhibits ColE7, the authors apply iterative rounds of mutagenesis and selection — first for affinity to ColE7, then for selectivity against ColE9 — achieving ~10⁵-fold affinity increase and 10⁸-fold selectivity increase. Structural and functional analysis of intermediates reveals that interface evolution proceeds through "generalist" intermediates and exploits latent, promiscuous binding configurations. Compensatory stabilizing mutations play a key role, paralleling enzyme evolution strategies.

This comprehensive review covers all aspects of colicin biology — from synthesis, release, and structural organization to receptor binding, import across the outer and inner membranes, and cytotoxic mechanisms. ## Regulated selection of germinal-center cells into the memory B cell compartment
**File:** `Regulated_selection_of_germinal-center_cells.txt`
**Subjects:** HyperMutation Systems, Germinal centers
**URL:** https://www.nature.com/articles/ni.3460

This paper investigates how memory B cells are selected from germinal center (GC) populations. Using a fate-mapping mouse model (S1pr2-ERT2cre-tdTomato), the authors show that GC B cells with lower-affinity BCRs preferentially enter the memory B cell pool. Mechanistically, these lower-affinity cells express higher levels of the transcriptional repressor Bach2, which promotes memory fate commitment. Bach2 haploinsufficiency reduces memory B cell generation. Bach2 expression inversely correlates with T cell help strength. The study proposes an instructive model: weak T cell help maintains Bach2 expression, predisposing GC B cells to become memory cells rather than plasma cells.

## SARS-CoV-2 variant prediction and antiviral drug design are enabled by RBD in vitro evolution
**File:** `SARS-CoV-2_variant_prediction_and_antiviral.txt`
**Subjects:** HyperMutation Systems
**URL:** https://www.nature.com/articles/s41564-021-00954-4

This study uses yeast surface display and iterative in vitro evolution to affinity-mature the SARS-CoV-2 spike RBD for ACE2 binding. Strikingly, the mutations selected in the lab (S477N, E484K, N501Y) match those found in real-world variants of concern (Alpha, Beta, Gamma), demonstrating that in vitro evolution can predict viral evolution. The best variant, RBD-62, achieves 1,000-fold higher ACE2 affinity (16 pM) than wild-type. RBD-62 functions as a competitive inhibitor of ACE2, blocking SARS-CoV-2 infection in vitro and reducing disease in a hamster challenge model. A 2.9 Å cryo-EM structure reveals the structural basis of enhanced binding.

## SARS-CoV-2 neutralizing antibody structures inform therapeutic strategies
**File:** `SARS-CoV-2_neutralizing_antibody_structures_inform.txt`
**Subjects:** HyperMutation Systems
**URL:** https://www.nature.com/articles/s41586-020-2852-1?utm_source=chatgpt.com

This structural biology study classifies SARS-CoV-2 neutralizing antibodies (NAbs) into four classes based on their binding modes to the spike RBD. Class 1 (VH3-53, short CDRH3) blocks ACE2 and binds only "up" RBDs. Class 2 blocks ACE2 but binds both up and down RBDs, with some bridging adjacent RBDs — notably, C144 locks the spike into a closed conformation via a hydrophobic CDRH3 tip. Class 3 binds outside the ACE2 site on both up and down RBDs. Class 4 binds a conserved cryptic epitope on up RBDs only. Mutant mapping shows escape mutations from one class don't affect others, supporting therapeutic cocktail strategies combining NAbs from different classes.

## Complete set of ORF clones of Escherichia coli ASKA library (A Complete Set of E. coli K-12 ORF Archive): Unique Resources for Biological Research
**File:** `Complete_set_of_ORF_clones.txt`
**Subjects:** N/A
**URL:** https://academic.oup.com/dnaresearch/article/12/5/291/350187?login=false

This paper describes the construction of the ASKA library — a complete set of individually cloned open reading frames (ORFs) from *E. coli* K-12. Each ORF is His-tagged at the N-terminus and GFP-fused at the C-terminus, cloned into a plasmid under IPTG-inducible control with tight repression by LacIq. SfiI restriction sites enable easy transfer to other vectors. The library supports systematic functional genomics including protein production and purification, localization studies via GFP fluorescence, protein–protein interaction analysis, and DNA microarray construction. Two versions exist: with and without GFP fusion, providing flexible resources for large-scale *E. coli* biology.
