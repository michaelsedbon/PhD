Summary 
Thirty-four 
hundred 
lambda 
phage 
clones 
containing 
segments 
of the E. coli chromosome 
were isolated 
and 
used to construct 
a 4700 kb long integrated 
restriction 
map for eight six-base-recognizing 
enzymes 
by a rapid

method. 
Our strategy 
was to measure 
the sizes of partial 
restriction 
enzyme 
digests 
by hy- 
bridization 
with a vector probe 
in a manner

to nucleotide 
sequencing. 
The data were sorted 
into

by a computer 
program 
and 
the 
boundary 
clones 
were further 
correlated 
with each other 
using 
a mass hybridization 
method. 
These clones 
can be ex- 
ploited 
for the isolation 
of any desired 
E. coli genes

Also, the strategy

Discussion 
Figure 
6 summarizes 
the restriction 
map data for a total 
of 3400 clones. There are still seven gaps that have not 
yet been covered by the clones isolated and analyzed 
thus 
far. Statistically, 
3400 clones containing 
15 to 20 kb of the 
chromosomal 
fragments 
should 
cover more than 99% of 
the E. coli genome. The length of each gap, therefore, 
may 
be only a few kilobases. 
One of the reasons that we were 
unable 
to clone these regions 
could be that certain 
DNA 
sequences 
are toxic to the cellular 
growth when they are 
cut off the rest of the chromosome 
and placed 
under the 
control 
of other 
replicons. 
For example, 
it has been 
reported 
that the gene Ipp, the structural 
gene for an outer 
membrane 
lipoprotein, 
cannot be cloned 
into a nonlyso- 
genie lambda phage vector (Nakamura 
and Inouye, 1979; 
K. Nakamura, 
personal 
communication). 
This gene is situ- 
ated at 36.5 min, which may explain 
why gap A at 1775 kb 
coordinates 
(Figure 6) exists. Comparison 
and alignment 
of the reported 
restriction 
map around 
this gene (Naka- 
mura et al., 1979) with ours indicate 
that gap A indeed 
includes 
the gene /pp. Therefore, 
we incorporated 
their 
published 
data and the gap was filled 
in as shown 
in 
Figure 
6. 
Another 
possibility 
for the existence of gaps may be that 
in some regions 
of the E. coli chromosome 
the cleavage 
sites for certain restriction 
enzymes do not exist for a long 
&etch, 
or, conversely, 
too many sites are congested 
in a 
short stretch. For example, the region containing 
oriC has 
been reported to be very rich in Sau3A cleavage 
sites and 
11 sites are known to exist in a stretch as short as 245 bp 
(Sugimoto 
et al., 1979). Therefore, 
some of these sites 
were cleaved 
in the partial digestion 
reaction with this en- 
zyme to yield short fragments 
which were accordingly 
ex- 
cluded from the Sau3A fragments 
of 15 to 20 kb in length. 
This may be the reason 
why gap B remained 
unclosed 
when only the Sau3A library clones were used for analy- 
sis. Later this gap was closed 
by a transducing 
phage 
lambda 
asn5 (Miki et al., 1978) and we found some candi- 
date clones for closing 
the gap in the EcoRl library. 
It is 
very probable 
that chromosomal 
regions 
containing 
too 
many Sau3A 
sites and at the same time too few EcoRl 
sites would escape the cloning 
strategy described 
above. 
The clones constructed 
by using a third or fourth enzyme 
must then be included 
to complete 
the map. In this con- 
nection, 
it should 
be noted that the libraries 
constructed 
in other laboratories 
(Daniels and Blattner, 1987) using re- 
striction 
enzymes 
different 
from those used in this work 
may complement 
our data and fill in the remaining 
gaps. 
Gap C is situated 
in the rpoBC region 
at 3515 kb coor- 
dinates (Figure 6). Fortunately,

available 
(the map constructed 
from the 
nucleotide 
sequence

ECRPOBC 
and ECR- 
RNBZ in the EMBL DNA Data Library; 
Boros et al., 1979) 
and, therefore, we have taken the data to close the gap as 
presented 
in Figure

were unable 
to clone this region.

en-

If 
an enzyme examined

both probes 
will hybridize 
the same chromosomal

striction

in 
the same manner

is the same as 
that conceived

sequencing 
(Church

has been adopted and analysis 
along this line is now in progress. 
The primary goal of this work was to construct a detailed 
restriction 
map covering 
the whole of the E. coli chromo-

this purpose 
to increase

the cloned

the Clarke-Carbon

(Clarke

The mobilities

The right end of the

the map data were converted

of cutting 
sites of restric- 
tion enzymes 
and overlapping 
clones 
were searched

(SDC, Tokyo) 
developed 
for shotgun 
sequencing 
of 
DNA. As the number 
of clones

to require 
faster

we had to 
use a central 
computer

a new program. 
This 
was 
achieved

RESBAS. 
The basic 
al- 
gorism 
of the dataalignment 
by this program 
is much the same as that

of DNA (Staden,

cept that it has to deal with the distance

the re- 
striction

the cleavage 
sites

B for BarnHI,

F for EcoRV, G for Bgll, Cl for

bases

parameters 
of that clone

its position 
from the 
left end of the group, etc. are recorded

access” 
file. 
A newly entered 
set of data was compared 
with the consensus 
data 
for the “groups” 
that had already 
been entered, 
sorted, 
and stored 
in 
the computer.

of a group was calculated 
for both the cleavage 
sites and their distances. 
When 
more than two- 
thirds 
at a position 
is occupied 
by the same 
cleavage 
site, it is taken 
as such. 
If not, that position 
is represented 
by a hyphen. 
The distances 
between 
the cleavage 
sites are averaged. 
The program 
reports 
a pos- 
sible overlap 
when newly entered 
data shows 
more than five consecu- 
tive matches 
with the consensus 
of a group, 
and, at the same 
time. 
when the distances 
between

leftward 
and rightward 
regions 
of that match also overlap

one or two cleavage 
sites 
into either 
the new data or the preexisting

can
