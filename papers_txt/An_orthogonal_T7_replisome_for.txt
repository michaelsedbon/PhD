SYNTHETIC BIOLOGY
An orthogonal T7 replisome  
for continuous hypermutation  
and accelerated evolution in E. coli
Christian S. Diercks1*, Philipp Sondermann1,  
Cynthia Rong1, Thomas G. Gillis1, Yahui Ban1, Celine Wang1,  
David A. Dik1,2, Peter G. Schultz1,2* 
Systems that perform continuous hypermutation of 
designated genes without compromising the integrity of the 
host genome can substantially accelerate the evolution of 
new or enhanced protein functions. We describe an orthogonal 
DNA replication system in Escherichia coli based on the 
controlled expression of the replisome of bacteriophage T7 
(T7-ORACLE). The system replicates circular plasmids that 
enable high transformation efficiencies and seamless 
integration into standard molecular biology workflows. 
Engineering of T7 DNA polymerase yielded variant proteins 
with mutation rates of 1.7 × 10−5 substitutions per base 
in vivo—100,000-­fold above the genomic mutation rate. We 
demonstrated continuous evolution using the T7 replisome by 
expanding the substrate scope of TEM-­1 β-­lactamase and 
increasing activity 5000-­fold against clinically relevant 
monobactam and cephalosporin antibiotics in less than 1 week.
Low organismal mutation rates [~10−10 substitutions per base pair (spb)] 
render mere passaging of cells under selective pressure an inefficient 
strategy for the diversification of genes on laboratory timescales (1, 2). 
Historically, directed evolution addressed this limitation by mutagenesis 
in vitro [e.g., error-­prone polymerase chain reaction (PCR)] followed by 
transformation of a microbial host with such diversified libraries for 
selection of phenotypic fitness (3–5). However, in vitro mutagenesis is 
time consuming and laborious and limits both the depth (rounds of 
mutagenesis) and scale (number of parallel experiments) of these experi-
ments. Although alternative randomization strategies (e.g., DNA shuf-
fling) have improved the efficiency of this process, the removal of ex vivo 
diversification by increasing in vivo mutagenicity enables continuous 
evolution at an accelerated pace (6).
Early efforts to increase in vivo mutation rates relied on chemical 
mutagens, engineered host DNA polymerases, and interference with 
host DNA repair machinery. The mutation rates that can be achieved 
according to these strategies are fundamentally limited by the large 
number of essential host genes that must be maintained faithfully to 
ensure survival (7–10). These limitations can be mitigated by using an 
orthogonal replication system where an error-­prone DNA polymerase 
exclusively maintains a dedicated episome (11, 12).
The first orthogonal replication system, OrthoRep, was developed by 
minimizing extranuclear plasmids of yeast that are replicated by an 
orthogonal cytosolic protein-­primed DNA polymerase (11, 12). Engineering 
of this DNA polymerase yielded clones with mutation rates of 10−5 spb 
that enable accelerated continuous evolution in vivo (13–16). Developing 
a mutagenic replication system in Escherichia coli will benefit from faster 
generation times and higher cell densities compared with yeast and en-
able markedly accelerated evolution experiments at the same mutation 
rate. Recently, a bacterial replication system, EcORep, based on the 
protein-­primed DNA polymerase of the lytic bacteriophage PDR1, was 
developed for E. coli (17). However, the mutation rates that can stably be 
maintained in the disclosed system (~2.0 × 10−7 spb) are only modestly 
enhanced over genomic levels (18). As such, the development of a highly 
mutagenic orthogonal replication system in E. coli remains an important 
challenge. In this work, we describe the development of T7-ORACLE (T7 
orthogonal replisome-assisted continuous laboratory evolution), a new 
orthogonal replication system for continuous hypermutation and acceler-
ated evolution in E. coli.
Establishing an orthogonal replication system based on the 
bacteriophage T7 replisome
The replication of the linear double-­stranded genome of T7 phage is initi-
ated by the priming of its replisome by T7 RNA polymerase (gp1) at a T7 
origin of replication (Fig. 1A) (19–23). T7 origins of replication rely on 
the orthogonality of the T7 RNA polymerase–promoter pair to E. coli 
RNA polymerase–promoter pairs for specificity in recruiting the repli-
some (Fig. 1B) (24). The orthogonal T7 transcript primes the T7 DNA 
polymerase (gp5), which further recruits the T7 helicase (gp4B) and 
helicase-­primase (gp4A) fusion protein to the transcription bubble. The 
host factor thioredoxin (trxA) binds to the T7 DNA polymerase and serves 
as a β-­sliding clamp. Replisome assembly initiates concerted leading and 
lagging strand synthesis, which further requires the presence of the T7 
single-­stranded DNA binding protein (gp2.5) for stabilizing the lagging 
strand replication loop (Fig. 1C) (23).
To create an orthogonal T7 replication system in E. coli, we gener-
ated a strain based on E. coli BW25113 in which all four essential T7 
replisome genes (gp1, gp2.5, gp4, and gp5) are supplied in trans using 
plasmid-­based expression systems (table S1) (25). We only expressed 
one of the two reading frames of gp4 (gp4A, helicase-­primase fusion), 
given the reported toxicity and nonessentiality of gp4B (helicase) 
(26, 27). Initial attempts at transforming this replisome strain with a 
circular plasmid carrying only a T7 origin of replication proved unsuc-
cessful, in agreement with literature precedent (28).
We reasoned that additional T7 genes are required to establish an or-
thogonal replication system. Although not strictly essential to T7 phage 
replication, T7 lysozyme (gp3.5) knockout phages, in which the gene is 
deleted, are severely replication deficient (29). T7 lysozyme has two func-
tions: cysteine hydrolase activity and inhibition of transcription by binding 
to T7 RNA polymerase (30, 31). The concerted expression of T7 lysozyme 
with the other replication proteins (gp1, gp2.5, gp4, gp5; fig. S1) suggests 
a regulatory mechanism in which lysozyme accumulation leads to a switch 
from transcription to replication in the phage life cycle (32). Inhibition by 
T7 lysozyme causes abortive cycling instead of the transcription of full 
genes. This produces short RNA “primers” for the T7 DNA polymerase to 
stimulate the assembly of the T7 replisome at the T7 origin (33–36).
To stimulate replication, we fused hydrolase-­deficient T7 lysozyme 
[C131S (Cys131→Ser)] to T7 RNA polymerase (37). To maximize this effect, 
we used the T7 origin of replication φOR (Fig. 1A), for which replication 
stimulation by T7 lysozyme was reported to be the highest (32). Finally, 
we chose a reported exonuclease-­deficient clone of T7 DNA polymerase 
(Δ28, T7 Sequenase 2) that had previously been used for T7 replisome-­
based in vitro replication assays (38–41). These changes enabled trans-
formation of a plasmid carrying a T7 origin of replication (pOR-­1; 
table S1), as well as stable replication for more than 2 weeks (Fig. 1, D and 
E). The strain showed robust growth [doubling time (Tdouble) of 29 min] 
and reached high cell densities [optical density at 600 nm (OD600) of 
7.2] in liquid culture (Fig. 1F). Mutation rates determined by fluctua-
tion analysis showed increased mutagenesis of the T7 origin plasmid 
(2.81 × 10−7 spb per generation) without affecting the host genomic muta-
tion rate (3.66 × 10−10 spb; table S4). The copy number of the T7 origin

Engineering T7 DNA polymerase mutants with increased 
mutation rate
Three distinct mechanisms account for the high fidelity (10−9 to 10−10 spb) 
of DNA replication. Base selection in the T7 DNA polymerase active site 
accounts for a fidelity of ~10−5 spb, 3′-­5′ exonucleolytic proofreading 
further increases fidelity by ~10−2 spb, and host mismatch repair is re-
sponsible for the remaining ~10−3 spb. Initially, we had used a 3′-­5′ proof-
reading exonuclease-­deficient version of T7 DNA polymerase (Δ28; 
Fig. 2A) (40–42). Comparison of this Δ28 mutant (3.31 × 10−8 spb) to wild-​
­type (WT) T7 DNA polymerase (1.4 × 10−9 spb) showed a 25-­fold differ-
ence in mutation rate (Fig. 2C).
Because DNA mismatch repair cannot be altered without compromis-
ing host fitness, we next targeted the fidelity of base selection in the 
polymerase active site to further increase mutagenicity. We selected 11 
sites of T7 DNA polymerase based on studies of homologous polymerases 
(10, 43) and generated single-­site–saturation libraries at each position 
(i.e., R429X, V443X, R444X, L479X, E480X, N520X, A521X, K522X, T523X, 
F524X, Y530X, P560X, and N611X; R, Arg; V, Val; L, Leu; E, Glu; N, Asn; 
A, Ala; K, Lys; T, Thr; F, Phe; Y, Tyr; P, Pro; X, any residue). A replisome 
strain deficient in T7 DNA polymerase (T7Rep-­Δgp5; table S2) was trans-
formed with these site-­saturation libraries and then challenged to revert 
a premature ochre stop codon in the TEM-­1 β-­lactamase gene (E26*ochre) 
on the T7 origin plasmid (pOR-­3; table S1) to restore resistance to the an-
tibiotic carbenicillin. We sequenced surviving clones using Illumina am-
plicon sequencing and subjected enriched DNA polymerase mutants to 
fluctuation analysis. This identified three mutations (N520M, P560V, and 
V443K; M, Met; Fig. 2B) that were mapped onto the Δ28 mutant. N520M 
likely decreases geometric constraints that contribute to base-­pairing se-
lectivity given its position in the O-­helix, which is in contact with incoming 
nucleotides in the active site. P560V is located in a flexible loop of the 
fingers domain that contacts this O-­helix. This loop serves as a “hinge” that 
opens and closes the active site for incoming nucleotides. Mutations in this 
domain likely affect base pairing by altering the kinetics of binding of the 
correct or incorrect nucleotides. V443K interacts with the DNA double helix 
at the replication fork and might decrease base-­pairing constraints by in-
creasing the flexibility at the replication fork. The identified mutations 
increased the mutation rate of exonuclease-­deficient T7 DNA polymerase 
to 6.75 × 10−7 spb for the best single mutant, 4.26 × 10−6 spb for the best 
double mutant, and 1.04 × 10−5 spb for the best triple mutant (Fig. 2C).
Next, we constructed a random mutagenesis library on top of the 
triple mutant and subjected it to the same reversion assay. This identi-
fied an additional seven sites for which single-­site–saturation libraries 
were constructed and screened (Y24X, P84X, M146X, N171X, A433X, 
L437X, Q585X, and R678X; Q, Gln; figs. S2 to S4). The most beneficial 
mutations identified were Y24F and Q585R (Fig. 2B). Notably, the Y24F 
mutation is in the exonuclease domain and was found to reverse 
growth deficiencies observed for the single, double, and triple mutants 
(figs. S5 to S10 and table S5) but did not show a change in mutation 
rate (1.1 × 10−5 spb). The last mutation was found in the Palm domain

We investigated the mutational spectrum of the quintuple mutant 
by Illumina sequencing using the NovaseqX platform (two replicates 
of 600 million paired-­end reads each) and analyzed the data using a 
custom analysis pipeline (44). The observed mutation rate across the 
two replicates was calculated as 1.93 × 10−5 spb, in good agreement 
with the results from fluctuation analysis. Heatmaps of the mutational 
spectrum were generated by calculating the mutational frequency of 
each nucleotide substitution across the two replicate datasets. We ob-
served a fairly unbiased mutational spectrum for both transition muta-
tions and transversion mutations (Fig. 2E).
Orthogonality to the genome was confirmed by fluctuation analysis 
using rifampicin resistance, which displayed a genomic mutation rate 
of 4.4 × 10−10 spb (Fig. 2D and table S4). Stability of the quintuple 
mutant was further confirmed by passaging the strain for 2 weeks, 
after which sequencing of the quintuple mutant of the replisome strain 
showed no changes in sequence and mutation rate (fig. S19). We fur-
ther determined that plasmid sizes of up to 13 kb in size can be main-
tained (pOR-­6; fig. S11 and tables S1 and S5) with comparable mutation 
rates (1.53 × 10−5 spb; table S3).
Continuous evolution of TEM-­1 β-­lactamase
Next, to demonstrate the utility of the mutagenic T7 system, which we 
have termed T7-ORACLE, for evolving protein function, we applied it to 
the continuous evolution of TEM-­1 β-­lactamase for an expanded 
substrate scope and improved activity against monobactam (az-
treonam) and cephalosporin antibiotics (cefotaxime, ceftazi-
dime, and cefepime; Fig. 3, A to C). An understanding of the 
mutational landscape of TEM-­1 β-­lactamase provides insight into 
the molecular basis for resistance in clinical isolates and is es-
sential for designing next-­generation β-­lactam antibiotics. To this 
end, T7rep-­7 was transformed with a pOR-­4 encoding the blaTEM-­1 
β-­lactamase gene (Fig. 3A and tables S1 and S2). Initial plating of 
the strain on antibiotic concentrations that approximate the mini-
mum inhibitory concentration of TEM-­1 against the respective 
antibiotics (0.1 μg ml−1 for aztreonam, cefotaxime, and cefepime; 
0.4 μg ml−1 for ceftazidime) served as the starting point for evo­
lution. For each experiment, 96 colonies were picked and pas-
saged under increasing concentrations of antibiotic to more than 
1000-­fold within 6 days (100 μg ml−1 for aztreonam, cefotaxime, 
cefepime; 500 μg ml−1 for ceftazidime; Fig. 3B). At the end of 
the experiment, the coding sequences of the β-­lactamase genes, 
as well as the pAmpR promoter region, were determined (Fig. 3D 
and figs. S14 to S16).
Convergence of mutations across replicates was observed for all 
antibiotics (Fig. 3, D and E), with all enriched mutations having 
previously been described in clinical isolates of extended spectrum 
β-­lactamases or laboratory evolution experiments (45, 46). Both 
the nature and the combination of mutations differed for the four 
antibiotics. In addition, the entire 96-­well plate for each evolution 
experiment was pooled after every passage and subjected to nano-
pore amplicon sequencing to establish a time course of when 
mutations occurred (Fig. 4 and figs. S17 and S18). For instance, the 
evolution of cefotaxime resistance (Fig. 4A) began to enrich for the 
G238S (G, Gly) substitution from the beginning, in agreement with 
the substitution being associated with increased hydrolysis of ce-
fotaxime. After passage 3, only clones that acquired a secondary 
mutation, E104K, survived. E104K is generally associated with mu­
tations G238S, R164H, or R164S (H, His) where it increases resis-
tance to cefotaxime, ceftazidime, or aztreonam, respectively (45, 47). 
Starting at passage 4, mutations that increased the strength of the 
promoter pAmpR were observed. Finally, over passages 4 to 6, one 
of four accessory mutations was enriched in most cultures (M182T, 
L49M, T264M, or G92D; D, Asp), all of which have been found as 
secondary mutations in clinical isolates or evolution experiments (45). 
M182T increases thermodynamic stability and helps prevent aggregation. 
Similarly, G92D and T264M were identified in evolution campaigns that 
select for stabilizing and compensatory mutations (45). The role of L49M 
is unknown, but the fact that it is observed frequently in the context of 
cefotaxime resistance supports an adaptive effect (45).
In the ceftazidime resistance time-­course experiment, mutations in 
the promoter region were observed from the outset (Fig. 4B). Ad­
ditionally, mutations R164H and R164S (improved active-­site access 
for β-­lactams with large side chains) were enriched from the begin-
ning, along with the aforementioned E104K secondary mutation. After 
passage 3, additional mutations (i.e., M182T, E240K, and D179G) were 
observed. E240K commonly occurred in combination with R164S and 
R164H, where it increases activity against ceftazidime and aztreonam 
(45,  47). D179G has been observed in evolution campaigns using 
ceftazidime as a selective agent (45). Over the last three passages, the 
percentage of D179G mutations steadily decreased, indicating that the 
mutation was being outcompeted at higher antibiotic concentrations. 
This 1-­week experiment perfectly recapitulated the vast body of work 
on extended-­spectrum β-­lactamases collected over decades, highlight-
ing the power of the T7 evolution system in interrogating complex 
evolutionary fitness landscapes.

evolution systems is that maintenance of a circular replicon enables high

shuffling). Under constant selective pressure, laboratory evolution tends 
to follow a path of least resistance across a hypothetical fitness landscape. 
This can be illustrated for the evolution of TEM-­1 for activity against 
cefotaxime, where replicate evolution experiments broadly converge 
to the same solution (Fig. 3, D and E). Conceptually, this can be described 
as evolution trajectories on a hypothetical fitness landscape being 
trapped in local fitness maxima as opposed to sampling the entire 
land­scape (Fig. 5, A to C). To overcome this limitation, we generated 
a site-­saturation library of TEM-­1 (E104X, Y105X, G238X, E240X, and 
R244X) covering the five residues that line the enzyme’s active site. 
Transformation of the library plasmid pOR-­5 (table S1) into the T7-­
ORACLE strain yielded full library coverage (3.4 × 107–member library). 
Ninety-­six clonal starting points were picked after initial selec-
tion (0.1 μg ml−1 cefotaxime) and passaged under increasing selection 
stringency. After 5 days, the replicate evolution experiments displayed 
vast diversity in their active-­site residues and differed from the solution 
for evolution of WT TEM-­1 (Fig. 5, D and E). Notably, although E104K 
was one of the most important mutations for cefotaxime resistance in 
WT TEM-­1, 16 distinct solutions were observed at this site in the evolu-
tion of the site-­saturation library (Fig. 5D). Similarly, 14 distinct solutions 
were observed at site 238, seven at site 105, and seven at site 240. No 
substitutions for R244 were identified, indicating that this residue is 
essential for activity. Secondary mutations related to stability and ex-
pression levels followed similar trends as for the WT enzyme evolution 
(e.g., pAmpR, M182T, L49M, T264M, and G92D). A second evolution 
experiment was carried out where, instead of 96 individual clones, the 
entire library was used as a starting point in each of the replicates. In 
this case, the active-­site mutations converged again, with >80% se-
quence identity at four of the five sites (E104F, Y105W, G238S, and 
R244R; W, Trp) and divergence only being observed at site 240 (Fig. 5, 
D and F). In addition, starting from the entire library allowed for in-
creasing the cefotaxime concentration even further (500 μg ml−1) with-
out any loss in viability, supporting the fact that evolution under these 
conditions can identify global maxima on the protein’s fitness land-
scape. This was further corroborated by the fact that the active-­site 
residues that converged in the experiment starting from the site-­
saturation library, when tested clonally, were found to be more active 
than the clones that converged from the evolution starting from WT 
TEM-­1 (fig. S20).
Discussion
We have established an orthogonal replication system in E. coli based 
on the replisome of bacteriophage T7. Directed evolution of the T7 
DNA polymerase for increased mutation rates yielded a replisome 
strain that can be continuously passaged at a mutation rate of 1.7 × 
10−5 spb. The combination of the high mutagenesis rate, the fast 
growth rate of E. coli, the high transformation efficiency, and the ease 
with which both the E. coli and the circular replicon plasmid can be 
integrated into standard molecular biology workflows differentiates 
T7-­ORACLE from OrthoRep and EcORep. In comparison to PACE, an-
other highly mutagenic continuous evolution system in E. coli, our 
system benefits from simplicity and the lack of specialized equipment 
that is required to run evolution campaigns, as well as from the ability 
to autonomously run continuous evolution campaigns in a large num-
ber of replicates (48, 49). One present limitation of T7-­ORACLE is the 
lack of inducible control of the replisome proteins, which would enable 
control over both the copy number of the replicon as well as modula-
tion of the mutation rate during evolution campaigns. To facilitate 
adaptation of the system, we constructed a strain in which all repli-
some genes are encoded on the genome (fig. S12 and tables S2, S3, and 
S5). Maintaining a circular replicon enables high transformation ef-
ficiencies of circular plasmids (2.4 × 1010 cfu μg−1) and thus the integra-
tion of prediversified libraries and continuous mutagenesis into a 
single workflow. This was illustrated by continuous evolution cam-
paigns in which we started with large active-­site libraries to explore 
broader fitness landscapes. Following this approach, we identified 
clones with improved activity relative to those found starting from the 
native enzyme. This will be particularly beneficial for evolving funda-
mentally new, as opposed to improved, activities, as well as for inves-
tigating vast fitness landscapes through divergent, as opposed to 
convergent, evolution campaigns.
