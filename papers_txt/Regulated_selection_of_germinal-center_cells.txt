Humoral memory relies on the development of memory B cells and 
long-lived plasma cells (PCs). Although memory B cells can be gen┬Ł
erated, at least to some extent, after the initial T cellŌĆōB cell interac┬Ł
tion before germinal-center (GC) formation, the majority of memory 
B cells responding to T cellŌĆōdependent antigens are thought to arise 
from the GC reaction1ŌĆō3.
Dynamic GC cell activity has been analyzed by live-imaging stud┬Ł
ies, which has established that GCs are structures in which B cells 
migrate between two zones, the light zone (LZ) and the dark zone 
(DZ)4ŌĆō6. Proliferation and somatic hypermutation occur in the DZ, 
followed by shuttling of B cells to the LZ, where they exit the cell cycle. 
Antigen-presenting follicular dendritic cells and follicular helper 
T cells reside in the LZ, where GC B cells expressing newly mutated 
BCRs capture antigen and internalize it for presentation to T cells. 
Subsequently, antigen- and T cellŌĆōdependent selection occurs in the 
LZ B cells, whereby positively selected B cells have the ŌĆśchoiceŌĆÖ of 
recycling to the DZ for further mutation or exiting the GC reaction 
as memory cells or PCs7,8.
One key unresolved question about the cell-fate ŌĆśdecisionsŌĆÖ made 
in the GC is how memory B cells are selected and generated. One 
study suggested that a memory B cell-selection pathway might oper┬Ł
ate differentially from the pathway that drives GC B cells into the PC 
compartment9. Two models, stochastic and instructive, have been put 
forward to explain the memory-selection mechanism. Studies of a wide 
variety of genetic perturbations have shown that, overall, distortions 
in the affinity composition of GC B cells are indistinguishable from 
those in memory B cells, while they often differ from those in PCs9. 
Such evidence suggests that memory B cells, unlike PCs, are selected 
stochastically from among GC B cells.
The alternative ŌĆśinstructive modelŌĆÖ postulates that selection into the 
memory B cell fate is regulated by a large number of means, such as 
extrinsic signals from T cells, including cytokines and cell-contact-
dependent signals, and affinity-based signals through the BCR. In 
support of this model, enhanced memory-cell generation has been 
observed when the T cellŌĆōB cell interaction is abnormal. For exam┬Ł
ple, mice lacking the receptor for interleukin 21 (IL-21) specifically 
on B cells show an accumulation of memory B cells concomitant 
with a reduction in GC B cells10,11. Such skewing of B cell popu┬Ł
lations has been explained by the idea that the mutant GC B cells 
receive aberrant help from T cells. Although the aforementioned data 
are informative, the evidence in support of each model is circumstan┬Ł
tial and contradictory.
Here, to address the selection and generation mechanisms of 
memory B cells, we first established a mouse model for marking GC 
B cells and their progeny within a given time window. Using these 
mice, we found that LZ GC B cells with BCRs of lower affinity had 
higher expression of the transcriptional repressor Bach2 than that 
of their counterparts with BCRs of higher affinity and were predis┬Ł
posed to enter the memory B cell compartment. Haploinsufficiency 
of Bach2 resulted in suppression of the generation of memory B cells,

Regulated selection of germinal-center cells into the 
memory B cell compartment
Ryo Shinnakasu1,2, Takeshi Inoue1, Kohei Kometani2,8, Saya Moriyama3,8, Yu Adachi4, Manabu Nakayama5, 
Yoshimasa Takahashi4, Hidehiro Fukuyama2, Takaharu Okada3,6,7 & Tomohiro Kurosaki1,2
Despite the importance of memory B cells in protection from reinfection, how such memory cells are selected and generated 
during germinal-center (GC) reactions remains unclear. We found here that light-zone (LZ) GC B cells with B cell antigen 
receptors (BCRs) of lower affinity were prone to enter the memory B cell pool. Mechanistically, cells in this memory-prone 
fraction had higher expression of the transcriptional repressor Bach2 than that of their counterparts with BCRs of higher 
affinity. Haploinsufficiency of Bach2 resulted in reduced generation of memory B cells, independently of suppression of the 
gene encoding the transcription factor Blimp-1. Bach2 expression in GC cells was inversely correlated with the strength of help 
provided by T cells. Thus, we propose an instructive model in which weak help from T cells maintains relatively high expression of 
Bach2, which predisposes GC cells to enter the memory pool.

RESULTS
Selection of GC B cells into the memory compartment
To identify the memory cells that had recently exited the GC reaction, 
we sought to make a transgenic mouse that could irreversibly mark the 
GC B cells in an inducible manner. To do this, we used the promoter of 
S1pr2, because among peripheral cells of the B lineage, both LZ and DZ 
GC B cells dominantly expressed this gene (Supplementary Fig. 1a). 
We established mice harboring a bacterial artificial chromosome with 
sequence encoding a tamoxifen-inducible Cre recombinaseŌĆōestrogen 
receptor 2 fusion protein (ERT2cre) inserted into exon 2 of S1pr2. 
These mice also had a transgene encoding the red fluorescent protein 
variant tdTomato with a loxP-flanked stop cassette controlled by the 
constitutively expressed Rosa26 promoter (called ŌĆśS1pr2-ERT2cre-
tdTomato miceŌĆÖ here) (Supplementary Fig. 1b). Hence, only cells 
expressing S1pr2 at the time of tamoxifen treatment gave rise to 
permanent expression of tdTomato. Indeed, we immunized S1pr2-
ERT2cre-tdTomato mice with nitrophenyl (NP)-chicken ╬│-globulin 
(CGG) in the adjuvant alum and found that short-term injection of 
tamoxifen into these mice labeled GC cells but not memory cells or 
PCs (Supplementary Fig. 1c).
To further validate the fate-mapping system, we gave the immu┬Ł
nized mice an injection of tamoxifen every 2 d. In this protocol, 
almost all the NP+IgG1+CD38ŌłÆ GC B cells in the spleen expressed 
tdTomato, whereas none of the activated non-GC CD38+GL7+ B cells 
were thus labeled (Fig. 1a). Labeled NP+IgG1+ memory B cells had 
somatic mutations in the gene segment VH186.2, which is the pre┬Ł
dominant immunoglobulin heavy-chain (Igh) variable (V) gene seg┬Ł
ment expressed by NP-specific B cells in C57BL/6 mice (Fig. 1a). 
Thus, we concluded that the labeled memory B cells were derived 
from irreversibly marked GC B cells.

We then analyzed VH186.2 sequences for the characteristic muta┬Ł

proliferative, whereas newly generated memory B cells exit the GC 
and promptly become quiescent. To make a more precise compari┬Ł

B cells, we limited the tamoxifen treatment to only 1 d, administered 
at day 9 after immunization. This strategy minimized the inherent 
problem that GC B cells are continuously proliferating and generating 
mutant BCRs. Replacement of Trp33 with Leu33 in the BCR (called 
ŌĆśW33L+ŌĆÖ here) is often used to pursue selection of the response to 
NP, as this mutant BCR exhibits an affinity for NP about a tenfold 
higher than that of a BCR without this substitution12. 63ŌĆō65% of the 
tdTomato+NP+IgG1+ LZ GC B cells were W33L+, whereas 36ŌĆō44% of

carrying more than five amino-acid substitutions in the ŌĆśW33G99ŌĆÖ

at a later time point, at day 18 after immunization (Supplementary

GC cells of relatively low affinity were favored for selection into the 
memory B cell compartment.

An important concept has emerged for memory CD8+ T cells in this 
context. For example, increasing amounts of the transcription factor 
T-bet facilitate increasing differentiation into effector CD8+ T cells. 
The counterbalancing transcription factor Eomes is highly homolo┬Ł

tempted to speculate that the same paradigm would also operate in 
B cells. We focused our analysis on the transcription factors IRF4 
and Blimp-1, both of which are known to foster PC differentiation. 
Published studies have suggested that the transcription factors Bach2, 
Pax5, Bcl-6, IRF8 and PU-1 block PC differentiation and thus would

LZ GC relative to that in high-affinity IgG1+ LZ GC cells (Fig. 2e). 
On the basis of the gene-expression profiles reported above, we con┬Ł

cycle and to undergo PC differentiation.

mature B cells leads to their de-differentiation into early uncommit┬Ł

own differentiation into PCs25. In contrast, haploinsufficiency of 
Bach2 increases the rate of PC differentiation in vitro and in vivo25,26. 
On the basis these findings, we considered Bach2 the more plausible 
candidate for influencing memory B cell fate. We hypothesized that 
higher Bach2 expression in IgG1+ LZ GC B cells would be advanta┬Ł
geous for the generation of memory B cells.

Effect of T cell help on Bach2 expression in GCs
Having demonstrated the importance of Bach2 in the generation of 
memory B cells, we sought to understand how Bach2 expression is 
regulated during the GC reaction. To define the kinetics of Bach2 
expression in antigen-specific B cells, we established Bach2 reporter 
mice with heterozygous expression of a red fluorescent protein via 
Bach2 regulatory elements (Bach2tdRFP/+)31, then immunized these 
mice with NP-CGG. Expression of the Bach2 reporter was transiently 
downregulated at day 4 after immunization25, then its expression was 
regained in GC B cells at day 7, albeit not to the level of expression in 
naive B cells (Fig. 6a). Expression of Bach2 became more heterogene┬Ł
ous and broad in NP+IgG1+ LZ GC cells by day 10 than at day 7, and 
the average overall Bach2 expression was decreased by day 10 (Fig. 6a). 
Those observations prompted us to investigate whether Bcl-6 might 
regulate Bach2 expression. We transduced Bcl-6 into Bcl-6-deficient 
B cells and found that overexpression of Bcl-6 upregulated the expres┬Ł
sion of Bach2 mRNA in vitro (Fig. 6b).
The differential expression of Bach2 in high-affinity LZ GC cell 
relative to its expression in low-affinity LZ GC cells suggested that the 
BCR and subsequent T cell help might affect expression of Bach2 on 
LZ GC cells. Given the importance of T cell help for the fate ŌĆśdecisionsŌĆÖ 
of GC cells, we next focused on the effects of such help, using an in 
vivo approach for delivering such help without cross-linking the BCR. 
Ovalbumin (OVA), a T cell antigen, was fused to a chimeric antibody 
specific for the dendritic-cell marker DEC205 (anti-DEC205ŌĆōOVA). 
This antibody can target DEC205-expressing wild-type B cells inde┬Ł
pendently of the BCR. The targeted B cells then process and present 
OVA peptides8, which leads to their efficient interaction with T cells 
and subsequent help provided by T cells. As a control for the pres┬Ł
entation of OVA peptides, we used anti-DEC205ŌĆōCS, in which an 
irrelevant antigen, Plasmodium falciparum circumsporozite protein, is 
fused to anti-DEC205. 10 d after immunizing wild type C57BL/6 mice 
with NP-OVA, we injected chimeric antibody into the mice and deter┬Ł
mined the abundance of Bach2 mRNA in NP+IgG1+ LZ GC B cells 
at day 12 (Supplementary Fig. 4e). In contrast to results obtained 
by injection of the control antibody (anti-DEC205ŌĆōCS), treatment 
with anti-DEC205ŌĆōOVA induced downregulation of Bach2 mRNA 
in NP+IgG1+ LZ GC cells in a dose-dependent manner (Fig. 6c). 
Conversely, in vivo inhibition of T cellŌĆōB cell interactions by blockade 
of CD40 enhanced Bach2 expression (Fig. 6d and Supplementary 
Fig. 4f). Furthermore, ex vivo experiments with NP+IgG1+ LZ GC 
B cells were carried out. In support of the in vivo results, anti-CD40 
induced dose-dependent downregulation of Bach2 (Fig. 6e).
According to the model currently in use, T cell help is the limiting 
factor for affinity maturation7,8. Therefore, the results presented above 
suggested that GC cells with relatively high Bach2 expression or low 
Bach2 expression corresponded to cells with low affinity for NP or 
high affinity for NP, respectively. Indeed, NP+IgG1+ LZ GC cells 
with high or low expression of Bach2 in NP-CGG-immunized mice 
expressed BCRs with relatively low or high affinity for NP, as assessed 
by single-cell analysis of VH186.2 sequences (Supplementary Fig. 5a). 
Moreover, the transcriptomes of cells with high or low expression 
of Bach2 were consistent with those of low- or high-affinity cells 
(Supplementary Fig. 5bŌĆōd). On the basis of these data, we concluded 
that Bach2 expression in LZ GC cells was inversely correlated with 
the strength of help provided to them by T cells.
Effect of Bach2 gene dosage on memory B cell development
To investigate whether the expression of Bach2 in GC cells affected 
their selection into the memory B cell compartment, we adop┬Ł
tively co-transferred two types of B cells that can be distinguished 
by congenic markers, Bach2+/+ERT2cre B1-8ge (CD45.1+CD45.2+) 
B cells and Bach2f/+ERT2cre B1-8ge (CD45.1+CD45.2ŌłÆ) B cells, 
into CD45.1ŌłÆCD45.2+ C57BL/6 host mice, then assessed the termi┬Ł
nal fate of each type of GC cell. We immunized the host mice with 
NP-CGG, then gave them injection of tamoxifen on days 7, 8, and 
9 after immunization and induced haploinsufficiency for Bach2 in 
Bach2f/+ERT2cre B1-8ge B cells, then treated the immunized recipi┬Ł
ent mice with the thymidine analog EdU for 3 d before analysis 
(Supplementary Fig. 6a,b). In this experimental setting, incorpora┬Ł
tion of EdU marks divided GC cells during the treatment period, 
before memory generation. The resultant memory B cells stop 
proliferating and thus retain their EdU labeling. To exclude the 
possibility that EdU+ memory B cells might be derived from non-GC 
proliferating B cells, we confirmed that during the period of day 10 to 
day 12, the majority of proliferating cells (>95%) were GC B cells 
and plasmablasts (Supplementary Fig. 6c). Despite the similar fre┬Ł
quency of EdU+ B1-8ge IgG1+ GC B cells in Bach2f/+ERT2cre mice and 
Bach2+/+ERT2cre mice, EdU+ Bach2f/+ERT2cre B1-8ge IgG1+ memory 
B cells were present at a significantly lower frequency than were EdU+ 
Bach2+/+ERT2cre B1-8ge IgG1+ memory B cells (Fig. 7). Consistent 
with that, in the anti-DEC205ŌĆōOVA experimental system described 
above, smaller amounts of anti-DEC205ŌĆōOVA enhanced the entry 
of GC cells into the memory-cell compartment (Supplementary 
Fig. 7aŌĆōc). Thus, we concluded that decreased expression of Bach2 
resulted in a decreased propensity of LZ GC cells to transit into the 
memory B cell compartment.
DISCUSSION
Here we found that the selection operates in an instructive manner: 
LZ GC cells with BCRs of relatively lower affinity were favored to be 
selected into the memory B cell compartment. As a potential mecha┬Ł
nism, we propose a model in which relatively weak help from T cells 
keeps Bach2 expression in LZ GC cells relatively high, a state advanta┬Ł
geous for entry into the memory B cell compartment.
Traditionally it has been thought that memory B cells are con┬Ł
tinuously produced through the period of GC activity32. In contrast, 
here we found that memory B cells were generated predominantly 
during the early GC reaction, consistent with published results33. 
One possible explanation for the traditional view is that memory 
B cells generated late during the immune response have a competi┬Ł
tive advantage over those generated earlier. Therefore, the memory 
cells generated late might be overrepresented. In the early GC period, 
BCRs on GC B cells are thought to be undergoing substantial diver┬Ł
sification, after which stringent affinity selection occurs by inter- or 
intra-clonal competition34. For example, in the case of the anti-NP 
response, key mutations begin to arise from day 9 to day 14 in GC 
cells35. Hence, selection of various GC B cells of relatively low affinity 
into the memory B cell compartment at an early time frame might 
provide a means with which to maintain poly-reactivity in the memory 
B cell pool.
It has been shown that once LZ GC cells with high-affinity BCRs 
are receiving ŌĆśstrongŌĆÖ help from T cells, they proliferate through tran┬Ł
sient upregulation of expression of the cell-cycle regulator c-Myc 
and thereby enter the DZ compartment15,36. In contrast, selection

the memory B cell pool.
Here we have identified Bach2 as a factor in the promotion of the 
entry of LZ GC cells into the memory B cell compartment; how┬Ł
ever, clearly, the concentration of Bach2 is only one of several factors 
underlying this selection process. Given the advantage of higher 
Bach2 expression in transition to the memory compartment, together 
with our observation that complete ablation of Bach2 after GC gen┬Ł
eration made the GC cells collapse (data not shown), we propose 
that a gradient of Bach2 is critical, whereby a small abundance is 
necessary for maintaining GCs and a large abundance is necessary 
for generating memory B cells. This mechanism probably operates 
predominantly at the early GC period, during which selection into the 
memory B cell compartment is actively occurring. At the late phase, 
LZ GC cells have relatively high expression of Bach2. We speculate 
that at this phase, the availability of antigen and subsequent T cell 
help is limited, which facilitates upregulation of Bach2 expression. 
Despite the high expression of Bach2, the majority of GC B cells 
were not selected into the memory B cell compartment at this phase. 
In this context, at least three possibilities can be envisaged. First, the 
size of the memory B cell pool at the late GC phase might preclude 
the selection of new memory B cells into this pool. Second, similar 
to the scenario proposed for CD8+ memory T cells18, as the GC cells 
proliferate extensively, they might lose their potential to become 
memory B cells in due time (often referred to as the ŌĆśdecreasing-
potential modelŌĆÖ). Finally, as proposed before5, the GC state in the 
early stage might differ qualitatively from that in the late stage and 
thus the cells might use different mechanisms for their subsequent 
fate ŌĆśdecisionsŌĆÖ.
How does Bach2 contribute to the generation of memory B cells 
in the early GC period? Given that during the transition from LZ GC 
cells to memory B cells, these cells are in the process of generating 
quiescent and long-lasting memory B cells, Bach2 probably executes 
anti-proliferative and/or pro-survival functions. As for an anti-
proliferative role, Bach2 might, for example, function by antagonizing 
c-Myc. On the other hand, Bach2 might provide a dose-dependent 
survival signal. Since Bach2 acts mainly as a transcriptional suppres┬Ł
sor21, we propose that suppression of the pro-apoptotic factors Bim 
or Puma by Bach2 might be a potential mechanism. Indeed, Bim 
and Puma were initially characterized as contributors to apoptosis 
of activated T cells and thereby dampened immune responses38. 
In addition, in Bim-deficient mice39, the size of IgG1+ memory B cell 
pool was increased.
It has been shown that in response to complex antigens such as 
influenza virus HA, GCs permit a wide range of intra- and inter-
clonal BCR affinities34. This allows the generation of GC B cells with 
a wider range of BCR affinities than previously thought, including 
ones of lower affinity in GCs. The ŌĆśpreferentialŌĆÖ selection of GC cells 
of lower affinity into the memory compartment, as we have shown 
here through the use of a simple anti-NP system, is probably applica┬Ł
ble to infection with influenza virus too, with the resultant generation 
of memory compartments with widely different BCR repertoires. 
This mechanism could provide a means with which to accelerate 
responses to recurrent infection by a hetero-subtypic influenza virus.
