avenue of adaptation in human evolution

Adaptation to environmental conditions occurs over diverse evolutionary time-
scales. In multi-cellular organisms, adaptive traits are often studied in tissues/ 
organs relevant to the environmental challenge. We argue for the importance of 
an underappreciated layer of evolutionary adaptation manifesting at the cellular 
level. Cell-autonomous adaptations (CAAs) are inherited traits that boost or-
ganismal ﬁtness by enhancing individual cell function. For instance, the cell-
autonomous enhancement of mitochondrial oxygen utilization in hypoxic environ-
ments differs from an optimized erythropoiesis response, which involves multiple 
tissues. We explore the breadth of CAAs across challenges and highlight their 
counterparts in unicellular organisms. Applying these insights, we mine selection 
signals in Andean highlanders, revealing novel candidate CAAs. The conservation 
of CAAs across species may reveal valuable insights into multi-cellular evolution.

Introducing cell-autonomous adaptation (CAA) 
Organisms adapt to a broad spectrum of environmental challenges, ranging from abiotic stresses 
like hypobaric hypoxia (see Glossary) and extreme temperatures to biotic pressures such as 
dietary shifts and emerging pathogens. In unicellular organisms, evolutionary adaptations primarily 
occur at the cellular level, exempliﬁed by mechanisms like heat-shock proteins, starvation re-
sponses, and antibiotic resistance [1–3]. Even in these simple organisms, adaptations can extend 
to community-level phenomena, such as bioﬁlm formation and colonial behavior [4,5]. In multicellular 
organisms, many adaptations are mediated by genes that enhance tissue or organ functionality, 
thereby conferring ﬁtness advantages to the organism by improving systemic ‘services’ such as 
digestion, oxygen delivery, and temperature regulation. These adaptations are foundational to 
multicellularity and the complex interplay of organismal systems. 
However, adaptations in multicellular organisms are not conﬁned to larger biological units like 
tissues, organs, or systems; they also occur at the level of individual cells. Therefore, here, we 
highlight and distinguish an often overlooked but crucial layer of evolutionary adaptation: cell-
autonomous adaptation (CAA). As the name implies, CAAs occur at the cellular level, in cell-
autonomous processes. The enhanced ﬁtness a CAA contributes to a multicellular organism 
results from advantages it confers to that organism’s individual cells (Figure 1). A CAA may manifest 
in a speciﬁc cell type or tissue (e.g., detoxiﬁcation of xenobiotics within cells exposed to the 
external environment). It may occur in cells throughout the body (e.g., cell-autonomous immune 
mechanisms). More operationally, if the cell that features a CAA is isolated from the body or 
organ (e.g., in cell culture) and is exposed to the condition that evoked its evolutionary emergence, 
the adaptation would still beneﬁt the cell (e.g., survival upon stress).

The concept of CAAs prompts intriguing evolutionary questions, as we will explore, about their 
emergence and prevalence in response to environmental challenges and how they compare to 
their non-cell-autonomous counterparts. Understanding the role of CAAs in evolution will reveal 
their contribution to the broader spectrum of adaptive responses across different scales of 
biological organization. 
Moreover, we expect CAAs to show universality across diverse lineages – as cells are the 
basic operational unit, from unicellular to multicellular life. Mechanisms that beneﬁt a unicel-
lular organism under stress are likely to beneﬁt cells within a multicellular organism under 
stress, ultimately improving the multicellular organism’s overall  ﬁtness. For example, certain 
cell-autonomous immune mechanisms in humans have evolutionary roots in bacterial im-
munity against phages, displaying their beneﬁt for both bacteria and humans [6]. This uni-
versality can allow us to investigate complex adaptations, such as those observed in humans 
adapting to extreme environments, through simpliﬁed systems like cell cultures or model 
organisms. 
We deﬁne postulates that an adaptation must fulﬁll to be established as a CAA (Box 1). We then 
explore manifestations of CAAs identiﬁed in the literature, spanning numerous environmental 
stresses. We identify novel CAAs by re-examining selection signals in high-altitude adapted 
populations while applying our approach for identifying CAAs. Our exploration highlights the evo-
lutionary signiﬁcance and prevalence of CAAs and sets the stage for research into their roles in the 
adaptive landscapes of diverse organisms. 
Glossary 
Cell-autonomous: a property of cells, 
processes, or functions that occur 
within an individual cell independently of 
surrounding cells or tissues. Cell-
autonomous mechanisms are intrinsic to 
the cell itself and do not rely on external 
signals or interactions with other cells for 
their execution. 
Chaperones: proteins that assist in the 
proper folding of other proteins, 
preventing misfolding and aggregation. 
Erythropoiesis: the process by which 
red blood cells (erythrocytes) are 
produced in the bone marrow, crucial for 
oxygen transport in the body. 
Genome-wide selection scans: a 
widely used method to detect signals of 
selection across the genome, often 
identifying genetic variants or regions 
associated with adaptation. 
Germline: the cells in an organism that 
are involved in reproduction and pass on 
genetic information to offspring. 
Hypobaric hypoxia: a condition  of  
reduced oxygen pressure, typically 
experienced at high altitudes. 
Polygenic adaptations: evolutionary 
adaptations that arise from the 
cumulative effect of small genetic 
changes across multiple genetic loci, 
rather than from a single genetic variant. 
Polymorphisms: the presence of two 
or more variants of a certain DNA 
sequence that can occur amongst 
different individuals or populations. 
Population branch statistic (PBS): a 
summary statistic that quantiﬁes allele 
frequency changes speciﬁc to  one  
population relative to two others and is 
commonly used to identify genomic loci 
potentially under selection. 
Somatic mutations: genetic alterations 
that occur in non-reproductive cells 
(somatic cells) of an organism. These 
mutations are not passed on to offspring 
but can affect the individual in which they 
occur, sometimes leading to diseases like 
cancer. 
Xenobiotics: chemical substances that 
are foreign to the biological system, 
including drugs, pollutants, and other 
synthetic compounds, requiring 
detoxiﬁcation processes in organisms.

Box 1. Three postulates for establishing a CAA 
We propose here three postulates that must be established in order to declare an observed genetic adaptation within 
an organism as a CAA. The postulates establish that the adaptation for coping with the environment is rooted in cells 
autonomously within the body, is beneﬁcial for both the cell and the organism, and is encoded via genes, attained by 
adaptive variants.

Hypoxia 
Limited oxygen availability constitutes a demanding environmental stressor, prompting the emer-
gence of various adaptive coping mechanisms across organisms. In humans, adaptations to 
hypoxia have been extensively studied in communities living at high altitudes, such as the Tibetan, 
Andean, and Ethiopian highlanders [7–12], and in free-diving populations [13]. A well-studied 
adaptation to hypoxia is altering levels of erythropoiesis via the EPAS1 gene [14–16]. EPAS1-
regulated erythropoiesis involves coordination between multiple cell types and tissues, making 
it clearly a non-CAA mechanism. Further, a changed number of erythrocytes does not inﬂuence 
ﬁtness of the individual erythrocyte. 
However,  a CAA  to  cope  with  hypoxia seems to have evolved. Comparison of  the  skeletal  
muscle of the Himalayan Sherpa, a people of Tibetan descent, to that of lowlanders revealed 
that Sherpa mitochondria have greater oxygen utilization efﬁciency and are better protected 
against oxidative stress [17]. While further studies are warranted to explore this trait across 
human populations, research on analogous adaptive strategies involving mitochondrial 
oxygen utilization and oxidative stress reduction in other species, such as deer mice and 
freshwater turtles, underscores the potential universality and functional importance of 
these adaptations [18,19]. In yeast, the isoform composition of cytochrome c oxidase is 
altered under hypoxic conditions, a change that enables the maximization of ATP production 
via the electron transport chain, while also reducing reactive oxygen species (ROS) genera-
tion [20]. Thus, similar to the mitochondria of Sherpa, the mitochondria of yeast can adapt to 
be more efﬁcient at oxygen utilization while minimizing harmful byproducts. This suggests 
that adaptations, from complex multicellular organisms like humans to simpler unicellular 
organisms like yeast, can share common principles at the cellular level, facilitating 
experimental studies. 
Cold climate 
Temperature is central in the physiology and survival of organisms and impacts both en-
dotherms, which internally regulate body temperature, and ectotherms, which rely on 
external sources to regulate body temperature. While endotherms likely employ a mix 
of CAAs and broader systemic responses, ectotherms’ reliance on CAAs may be 
more pronounced due to their direct thermal coupling with their surroundings. For ex-
ample, TRPM8, a cold-perception ion channel, exempliﬁes a non-CAA found in endo-
therms like humans and is central in thermoregulation [21]. Conversely, ectotherms 
may lean more on CAAs such as thermosensitive RNA structures, antifreeze proteins, 
or cold-adapted enzymes [22–25]. 
A CAA evident in both endothermic and ectothermic organisms is cellular thermogenesis. This 
form of heat production often involves futile cycles of metabolic pathways operating in reverse 
to dissipate energy as heat rather than as metabolic products. The Thyroid Adenoma Associated 
(THADA) gene regulates one such futile cycle and has been implicated in cold adaptation in 
various lineages, including humans, ﬂies, and plants (Box 2) [26,27]. Sea otters use mitochondrial 
uncoupling to generate heat in their skeletal muscle, utilizing cellular thermogenesis to survive 
cold waters [28]. An exciting example of a broad set of adaptations to low temperature is 
found in the woolly mammoth genome [29]. While many of the mammoth’s evolved genes are 
involved in non-CAAs (e.g., hair development and body size), some adaptations are likely CAAs, 
such as those involved in cellular thermogenesis (i.e., TET1) and DNA repair (i.e., BRCA1). Although 
most cells in endotherms do not directly experience cold due to thermoregulation, subcutaneous 
beige fat cells demonstrate a cell-autonomous ability to both sense and respond to cold via mito-
chondrial uncoupling, reﬂecting yet another example of this CAA to cold [30].

Another essential cellular adaptation is the regulation of membrane ﬂuidity in response to cold, 
which can increase rigidity and impair cellular function [31]. This adaptation often involves 
modifying fatty acid composition, a strategy seen from bacteria to multicellular animals, including 
ﬁsh, crustaceans, and even in reindeer hooves submerged in snow [32–36]. 
In summary, while ectotherms likely depend more on CAAs due to their lack of systemic thermo-
regulation, it seems that CAAs are crucial for protecting all organisms from extreme temperatures.

Approach for identifying novel CAAs 
In this section, we outline a systematic approach to reveal novel CAAs from genome-wide 
selection scans. Selection scans identify genetic variants potentially linked to adaptation 
[7,9,11,13]. Most genetic variants fall in noncoding regions, posing a challenge for identifying 
adaptive genes. Therefore, reasonably, genes highlighted in selection scan studies are often 
those involved in speciﬁc tissues/organs related to the environmental condition. However, this 
overlooks potential CAAs. For instance, high-altitude adaptation research has focused on the 
adaptive gene EPAS1, with its role in erythropoiesis and expression in the vascular endothelium 
and kidneys [37,38]. Yet, adaptations enhancing cellular oxygen metabolism or bolstering the 
cell’s stress response are compelling instances of CAAs that warrant equal attention. 
To systematically identify novel CAAs, we propose integrating the concept and awareness of 
CAAs into multicellular adaptation studies. Using genome-wide selection scans to identify both 
CAA and non-CAA candidates, we can then proceed with a targeted analysis to distinguish 
CAAs from non-CAAs. 
Our approach begins by assessing whether a gene’s deletion (or depletion) reduces cellular 
ﬁtness in culture under conditions that simulate the environmental challenge, indicating the 
gene’s potential as a CAA (as stated in Postulate 3; Box 1). Next, we examine whether the 
gene exhibits deep evolutionary conservation. We posit that CAAs often manifest in genes 
with deep evolutionary conservation, such as those shared between mammals and unicellu-
lar organisms, since the latter must mainly adapt through cell-autonomous mechanisms. 
Comparable gene functions or pathways conferring adaptive advantages in both mammals 
and unicellular organisms lends support to the candidacy of a CAA. Finally, we consider 
the breadth of the gene’s expression in the body. We suggest that CAAs often, although 
not exclusively, occur via genes that are broadly expressed across tissues and organs, 
indicating a more fundamental, cellular-level function. 
By applying these ﬁltering steps to genes associated with selection signals, we aim to reﬁne the 
distinction between CAAs and non-CAAs, thereby enriching our comprehension of adaptation 
in multicellular organisms. 
Box 2. THADA: a CAA conserved across yeast, plants, and animals 
An intriguing aspect of CAA is that it can be shared, due to common descent or to convergence, across species and even bio-
logical kingdoms. A potential example of an across-kingdom adaptation is the gene THADA. THADA is associated with obesity 
and metabolism in humans [27] and is a top gene under positive selection in humans living in cold Arctic climates [52]. THADA 
was also recently detected in a selection scan performed on cold-adapted chickens as a candidate adaptive gene [76]. This gene 
has orthologs across distant lineages, including non-vertebrates such as fruit ﬂies, plants, and even yeast. Moraru et al. investi-
gated THADA in Drosophila melanogaster and showed that it binds the  sarco/ER Ca2+ ATPase (SERCA) and functions as a met-
abolic uncoupling agent [27]. By binding SERCA, THADA enables ATP hydrolysis to occur but disrupts its coupling to Ca2+ ion 
transport. This results in a futile cycle that produces heat and is proposed to provide beneﬁt in thermoregulation in cold conditions. 
Subsequently, THADA was found to have cold-resistant properties in Arabidopsis thaliana as well [26]. This demonstrates 
that the cold sensitivity resulting from deletion of this gene is preserved across distant species with different organ systems. 
Finally, there is a yeast ortholog of THADA called TRM732. Its role in cold stress is unknown, but it is involved in tRNA meth-
ylation. Notably, the yeast null mutant can be functionally complemented by the human gene THADA, indicating that its 
function may be conserved between humans and yeast [77]. 
In the case of CAAs when function is conserved across lineages, we can extrapolate across vast evolutionary distances. 
For instance, mutations in the human gene THADA are associated with thyroid adenomas, type 2 diabetes, and polycystic 
ovary syndrome [78,79]. This underscores the potential of investigating orthologs of genes such as THADA in simpler 
eukaryotes to enhance our understanding of human diseases.

However, there are some challenges to keep in mind in this approach. While selection scans have 
uncovered numerous true and important adaptive signals, they also produce noisy signals that are 
not always reproducible across studies, presenting a challenge both within evolutionary genomics 
and in our approach for identifying CAAs. Additionally, selection scans are often optimized for detect-
ing selective sweeps, making it difﬁcult to capture polygenic adaptations, which are increasingly 
recognized as a major mode of adaptation. Acknowledging these limitations, our approach aims to 
improve the selection of CAA candidates by incorporating additional criteria to ﬁlter for higher-
conﬁdence adaptations and by emphasizing the importance of experimental follow-ups. 
Applying our approach to discover novel adaptations in Andean highlanders 
To demonstrate the utility of using this approach to uncover novel CAAs, we apply it to a case 
study of the Andean highlands. Crawford et al. scanned genomes of high-altitude adapted 
Andeans and detected signals of positive selection, revealing strong evidence for selection on 
cardiovascular function and development [9]. Building on their valuable ﬁndings, we reanalyze 
their data to explore additional candidate adaptive genes, particularly those involved in cell-
autonomous processes, which may not have been the primary focus of the original study. They 
used a modiﬁed version of the population branch statistic (PBS) to test for genomic loci that 
have differentiated allele frequencies in Andean highlanders compared with other populations, 
resulting in a PBS value for each locus. Here, we consider the genomic loci with the top 0.1% 
PBS values as regions with the strongest signals of selection. Using the Geneyx Analysis platform 
[39], the top regions could be associated with genes if they either overlap with the genes or with 
their regulatory elements. We then used the Geneyx phenotyping algorithm to scrutinize these 
top genomic loci and their associated genes for connections in the literature to keywords related 
to high altitude adaptation (e.g., ‘high altitude’, ‘UV radiation’, ‘altitude sickness’). Reassuringly, 
the genomic loci with the strongest connections to high-altitude keywords were those associated 
with EPAS1 and EGLN1, two well-established high-altitude adaptive genes. This analysis revealed 
numerous additional associations with potentially adaptive genes but without distinguishing 
between CAAs and non-CAAs. Our next step aimed to pinpoint novel CAAs among the hits by 
applying the approach we proposed earlier: assessing for ﬁtness effects in cell culture, deep 
homology, and broad expression across tissues. With this ﬁltering approach, we identiﬁed two 
novel genes/gene families that were previously unreported as adaptive genes in high-altitude 
human populations and that show strong potential as CAAs: HSP70 and GTF2H5. 
The case of HSP70 
The HSP70 gene family, encoding chaperones, is integral to the cellular stress response and 
pivotal for cell survival under stress [40]. Applying our approach to this gene family revealed a 
ﬁtness effect in cell culture, deep homology, and broad tissue expression. Under stressful condi-
tions (e.g., UV radiation and hypoxia), overexpression of HSP70 genes in human and rodent 
cells boosts their survival, while silencing or inhibiting HSP70 results in increased cell death 
[41–43], aligning with Postulate 3 (Box 1). Moreover, HSP70 has deep evolutionary roots, with 
orthologs in bacteria, and many genes in the HSP70 gene family show broad expression across 
tissues [44]. 
HSP70 displays signals of selection in Andeans. Two enhancers associated with the gene 
HSP70-HOM overlap with the top 0.1% of genomic loci under selection (EH38E2458651, 
EH38E2458648). This association with positive selection aligns with Postulate 2, which states 
that the adaptation should be detected by signals of selection if it is beneﬁcial at both the cellular 
and organismal levels (Box 1). Further, polymorphisms in HSP70-HOM and other HSP70 genes 
are associated with susceptibility to chronic mountain sickness in Han Chinese individuals 
[45,46], supporting a role in human health and adaptation to high altitudes. Experimental

The case of GTF2H5 
Central to the transcription-coupled nucleotide excision repair mechanism, GTF2H5 is criti-
cal in addressing UV-induced DNA damage [49].  GTF2H5  is  essential for  human cell survival  
under UV stress [50], a feature that aligns with Postulate 3 (Box 1). The conservation of 
GTF2H5 between humans and yeast, and its widespread tissue expression, indicates its 
fundamental cellular role [51]. Seven enhancers associated with GTF2H5 overlap with the 
top 0.1% of genomic loci under selection in Andean highlanders (EH38E2519468, 
EH38E2519471, EH38E2519637, EH38E2519643, EH38E2519644, EH38E2519645, 
EH38E2519647). This suggests an evolutionary advantage for organismal ﬁtness, alongside 
its cellular signiﬁcance, aligning with Postulate 2. While both HSP70 and GTF2H5 align with 
Postulates 2 and 3, Postulate 1 is not speciﬁc to a gene; rather, it requires that cells from 
adapted individuals, encompassing all encoded adaptations, demonstrate greater resilience 
to relevant stresses compared with cells from non-adapted individuals. Testing this premise 
would involve isolating cells from high-altitude populations, such as endothelial or skin cells, 
and exposing them to hypoxia or UV radiation, respectively. Enhanced survival in these cells 
would demonstrate Postulate 1. GTF2H5 and HSP70 are thus compelling candidates for 
experimental follow-up to further our understanding of evolutionary adaptation. 
Concluding remarks 
Here, we have introduced CAAs as a distinct class of evolutionary adaptations operating at the 
cellular level. Unlike non-CAAs that involve various organs or tissues, CAAs can transcend 
deep phylogenetic boundaries, potentially dating back to unicellular organisms, due to the shared 
cellular unit. This characteristic suggests a foundational role in evolutionary history. This frame-
work enriches our understanding of adaptation to new environments and poses intriguing ques-
tions about the evolutionary trajectories of complex life forms (see Outstanding questions). Might 
CAAs represent an evolutionary bridge, facilitating emergence of more intricate biological  
mechanisms? Could cell-autonomous immune defenses preserved from prokaryotes [6] have  
set the stage for the development of the sophisticated adaptive immune system in multicellular 
organisms? Perhaps CAAs and non-CAAs evolve over different timescales. For instance, CAAs 
might adjust more rapidly to environmental shifts through changes in one or few genes, 
whereas non-CAAs could require broader genetic alterations that take longer to establish. 
Conversely, the fundamental nature of cellular processes might make such adaptations more 
challenging to optimize, possibly entailing signiﬁcant evolutionary trade-offs. Perhaps combin-
ing cell-autonomous solutions with non-cell-autonomous ones might accelerate adaptation to 
new environmental conditions. Beyond the speed of adaptations, could the potential for ﬁtness 
increase differ between CAAs and non-CAAs? A deeper understanding of these dynamics can 
shed light on the evolution of multicellularity and how organisms adapt across different scales. 
Additionally, adaptations may manifest not only at the genetic level but also through physiolog-
ical processes. Like non-CAAs, CAAs may have a dual role in immediate and heritable 
responses to environmental challenges. Some of the CAAs discussed here, including cellular 
thermogenesis and membrane ﬂuidity, are adaptive both at the physiological and genetic 
level [27,33,52–54].

Moreover, the classiﬁcation of adaptations into CAAs and non-CAAs opens new avenues in 
experimental studies, as some evolutionary strategies are universally conserved across life forms. 
This conservation provides an opportunity to understand complex mammalian adaptations, 
including those in humans, through cell culture or simpler model organisms, separate from the in-
tricate interplays between organs and tissues. Unlike the challenges associated with non-CAAs, 
which often require animal models to dissect effects in the whole organism, studying CAAs within 
their biological context holds substantial potential for biomedical research (Figure 3). This distinction 
facilitates a deeper understanding of how cellular processes underpin broader adaptive traits, 
enhancing our ability to apply these insights in medical and evolutionary biology contexts. 
Additionally, the CAA conceptual framework can contribute practically to genomic research. 
Numerous genomes have been sequenced from populations adapted to extreme conditions 
such as high altitudes [7,9,11], cold climates [52], or breath-holding in divers [13]. Often, attention 
is drawn to genetic variants affecting non-CAA genes expressed in relevant organs or tissues. 
Yet, potential CAAs like those inﬂuencing cellular metabolism, stress responses, or repair path-
ways should not be overlooked. Attending to CAAs, using our suggested approach, could enrich 
strategies for studying adaptations in multicellular organisms confronting new environmental 
challenges. 
Comparing the ﬁtness of cells from adapted versus non-adapted individuals under laboratory 
conditions, such as cells from high-altitude-adapted populations exposed to hypoxia, could 
yield insights that can further advance our understanding of CAA. Investigating how speciﬁc 
genetic modiﬁcations affect cell ﬁtness under these conditions could uncover previously un-
identiﬁed adaptations, offering fresh perspectives on how cells, and hence organisms, 
adapt to their environments. Finally, systematically exploring the prevalence of CAAs across 
diverse environments and populations can enhance our understanding of the evolutionary 
process.

environments. Further, since the cellular unit is shared across species, it enables comparisons across lineages.
