SUMMARY
Antibody responses are characterized by increasing afﬁnity and diversity over time. Afﬁnity maturation oc-
curs in germinal centers by a mechanism that involves repeated cycles of somatic mutation and selection.
How antibody responses diversify while also undergoing afﬁnity maturation is not as well understood.
Here, we examined germinal center (GC) dynamics by tracking B cell entry, division, somatic mutation,
and speciﬁcity. Our experiments show that naive B cells continuously enter GCs where they compete for
T cell help and undergo clonal expansion. Consistent with late entry, invaders carry fewer mutations but
can contribute up to 30% or more of the cells in late-stage germinal centers. Notably, cells entering the
germinal center at later stages of the reaction diversify the immune response by expressing receptors that
show low afﬁnity to the immunogen. Paradoxically, the afﬁnity threshold for late GC entry is lowered in the
presence of high-afﬁnity antibodies.
INTRODUCTION
During immune responses, B lymphocytes undergo clonal
expansion and afﬁnity maturation in microanatomic structures
called germinal centers (GCs) that are divided into light and
dark zones. In this reaction, cycles of B cell division coupled
to somatic mutation in the dark zone are followed by migration
to the light zone where newly developed receptors are tested
for binding to antigen displayed on follicular dendritic cells.
B cells expressing receptors that can capture the antigen in
the light zone are selected by T follicular helper cells (Tfh) to
return to the dark zone for additional division and mutation.
These cycles of mutation and selection are critical to the devel-
opment of high afﬁnity antibodies in response to infection or
vaccination.1–3
In
parallel to
increasing
afﬁnity, antibody
responses also become more diverse over time, a phenomenon
that was ﬁrst described by Landsteiner and later by Eisen but re-
mains poorly understood.4,5
Experiments in mice immunized with haptens initially sug-
gested that only 2–3 B cells enter a given GC, thereby limiting di-
versity,6–8 but more recent analysis revealed that tens and
possibly as many as one hundred different B cells can enter
the same GC and compete for T cell help.9–11 Entry into the
GC is gated by antigen capture, which is dependent on B cell
receptor (BCR) afﬁnity, and subsequent processing and presen-
tation to T helper cells.9,12–15 Thus, the competition for entry
resembles the Darwinian process that occurs within GCs in
that it favors B cells that are present at higher frequency in the
repertoire
and/or
express
higher
afﬁnity
receptors.11,16–18
Consistent with this notion, even low afﬁnity cells that would
not otherwise enter the GC are able to do so in the absence of
competition.9,12,19
Once
established,
GCs
are
dynamic
open
structures
wherein naive B cells trafﬁc through the light zone.20,21 The
open nature of the structure suggested that B cells with the
appropriate cognate receptors might also be able to join
ongoing GC reactions.20 Consistent with this idea, adoptive
transfer experiments with high afﬁnity transgenic B cells,9,22,23
or B cells pre-loaded with antigen,24 showed that naive B cells
expressing high-afﬁnity receptors could join ongoing GC
reactions. However, whether the GC continuously recruits
naive B cells under physiologic circumstances and how this
might contribute to the diversity of immune responses are
not known.

Here, we describe lineage tracing experiments in which the
fate of polyclonal naive B cells is tracked during GC responses.
These data indicate that GCs continuously recruit B cells and
help to clarify how these late invaders help diversify the immune
response. Notably, high-afﬁnity antibodies lower the afﬁnity
threshold for entry resulting in GC invasion by B cells expressing
receptors with low to no measurable afﬁnity for the immunogen.
RESULTS
Phenotypic evolution of dividing B cells during naive to
GC transition
Upon BCR engagement with antigen, activated B cells migrate to
the T-B border where they interact with cognate T follicular help-
er (Tfh) cells.1–3,8,25 Tfh help is differentially provided based on
the amount of cognate peptide-major histocompatibility com-
plex (pMHC) presented by the activated B cells.9,26
To characterize activated B cells in their intermediary, pre-GC
state, we examined the relationship between expression of IgD,
CD38, CD95, GL7, activation induced cytidine deaminase (AID),
and cell division. 4-hydroxy-3-nitrophenyl acetyl (NP)-speciﬁc
B1-8hi AicdaGFP/+ B cells were labeled with CellTrace Violet
(CTV) and adoptively transferred into mice that were subse-
quently immunized with NP coupled to ovalbumin (NP-OVA) (Fig-
ure 1A).27,28 Because pairing of the knockin B1-8hi heavy chain
with Igl generates a high-afﬁnity receptor for NP, we examined
the phenotype of Igl+ B1-8hi CD45.1+ cells 7 days after immuni-
zation (Figures 1B and 1C). GL7 was upregulated in the ﬁrst
rounds of cell division (Figures 1B and 1C). Loss of naive
markers, CD38 and IgD, and acquisition of corresponding GC
markers, CD95 and AID, occurred more gradually (Figures 1B
and 1C), with a mature GC phenotype emerging after 6 divisions
(Figures 1B and 1C). Thus, activated B cells are GL7+CD95low
IgD+CD38+AIDlow and adopt a GC phenotype over the course
of 6–8 cell divisions.
To determine whether activated B cells are present throughout
the immune response, we combined AicdaGFP/+ with a tetracy-
cline-repressible cell division reporter (VavTg Col1amCherry).29–31
Administration of doxycycline represses constitutive histone
H2B-mCherry (H2B-mCh) expression and subsequent cell divi-
sion results in H2B-mCh dilution.31 Using this combination of re-
porters, activated B cells can be distinguished from GC B cells
based on cell surface markers, AID expression, and their reser-
voir of undiluted H2B-mCherry (Figures S1A and S1B).
AicdaGFP/+ VavTg Col1amCherry mice were immunized with an
HIV-1 antigen, TM4-Core, treated with doxycycline 0, 1, 2, or
4 weeks after immunization, and GC and activated B cells iso-
lated from draining lymph nodes were quantiﬁed 1–2 weeks later
(Figures 2A and 2B). Small numbers of activated B cells (CD38hi
IgDhi GL7hi CD95hi H2B-mChhi) were present throughout the
6-week observation period, peaking in parallel with the GC
(Figures 2B, S1C, and S1D). The number of activated B cells
was on average 20-fold lower than the number of GC cells
(Figure 2B).
To determine whether B cells enter the activated compartment
throughout the immune response, we performed pulse chase
experiments by combining AicdaCreERT2/+R26eYFP/+ with VavTg
Col1amCherry (Figures 2C and 2D). Administration of tamoxifen
to these mice labels activated B cells and GC B cells express-
ing AID whereas doxycycline treatment tracks cell division.
AicdaCreERT2/+R26eYFP/+VavTgCol1amCherry mice immunized with
TM4-Core received doxycycline at the time of immunization,
tamoxifen on days 0 or 7 or 12 or 19, and were assayed on
days 9, 14, or 21 (Figure 2C).
When activated cells were labeled by tamoxifen on day 0, only
0.4% remained mCherryhi on day 9. In contrast, 2% were
mCherryhi when tamoxifen was administered on day 7 and as-
sayed on day 9 (p < 0.0001, Figures 2D and S1E). Similar results
were obtained when tamoxifen was administered on days 7 or
12, and the analysis performed on day 14 (0.3% and 1.5%,
p <0:0001) or after doxycycline administration on day 12 and
tamoxifen on days 12 or 19 and assayed on day 21 (0.5% and
1.2%, p = 0.0027) (Figure 2D). In all instances, activated B cells
that had undergone limited numbers of division were found when
the assay was performed 2 days after tamoxifen and to a much
lower extent after 7 or 9 days (Figure 2D). These data indicate
that follicular B cells enter and exit the activated B cell compart-
ment throughout the GC reaction.
Antigen binding by GC B cells
To analyze the evolution of antigen binding among GC B cells,
we immunized mice with TM4-Core in alum and assayed the
popliteal lymph nodes 12, 27, and 42 days after immunization
(Figures 2E and 2F). In the absence of immunization, GC B cells
were nearly undetectable in the lymph node (Figure S2). In the
immunized cohort, the number of GC B cells remained stable be-
tween days 12 and 27 and decreased signiﬁcantly by day 42
(p < 0.05, Figure S2). Notably, the proportion of TM4-Core bind-
ing cells as measured by ﬂow cytometry also decreased,
accounting for 45%, 37%, and 22% of GC cells on days 12,
27, and 42 after immunization, respectively (Figures 2E and 2F).
Decreased binding to TM4-Core could be due to the accumu-
lation of deleterious mutations. To examine this possibility, we
sequenced the antibodies produced by individual TM4-Core+
and TM4-Core GC B cells (Figure 2G). The number of immuno-
globulin (Ig) mutations increased signiﬁcantly over time in the
antigen-binding TM4-Core+ GC B cell population (Figure 2G).
The number of mutations in the antibodies expressed by GC B
cells that did not bind TM4-Core also increased between days
12 and 27 but did not increase further between days 27 and 42
(Figure 2G). Moreover, the number of mutations on days 27
and 42 was higher in the antigen-binding population than that
in the non-binding population (p < 0.0001; Figure 2G). Thus,
TM4-Core-binding B cells found by ﬂow cytometry in the GC
after 42 days have a higher number of mutations than their
non-binding counterparts. Therefore, mutations do not appear
to account for the increasing number of non-antigen binding
GC B cells over time.

experiments using S1pr2-ERT2cre mice.32 S1pr2 is expressed
in the activated B cell compartment and in GC B cells but not
in naive B cells.33 Tamoxifen treatment of S1pr2-ERT2cre

Notably, despite their increased representation in the GC, un-
labeled tdT cells were signiﬁcantly less mutated than their tdT+
counterparts (Figure 3I). Because accumulation of somatic mu-

division. One potential explanation for the increasing representa-

the end of the tamoxifen labeling window. In this model, the in-
crease in the tdT TM4-Core population over time would be
due to late GC entry by naive B cells.
Naive B cells invade and clonally expand in existing
germinal centers
S1pr2CreERT2/+ R26tdT/+ labeling is incomplete, and therefore,
cells that remain unlabeled after tamoxifen injection could be
early GC entrants that failed to delete the lox-stop-lox cassette
in the R26tdT/+ allele. To address this shortcoming, we utilized
a complementary fate-mapping approach. CD55 was identiﬁed
as a gene that is highly expressed in naive and memory but
not GC B cells (Figures S4A–S4D).36 A conditional reporter
strain, Cd55CreERT2/+ R26tdT/+, was produced wherein tamoxifen
treatment speciﬁcally labels naive and memory B but not GC
cells (Figures S4A–S4E).
To validate the CD55-based fate-mapping approach, we
treated mice with tamoxifen and determined the speciﬁcity and
degree of labeling. After 4 days, 28%–34% of the naive B cell
compartment
is
labeled
in
secondary
lymphoid
organs
(Figures S4F–S4H). Consistent with CD55 expression data, GC
cells in Peyer’s patches were not labeled (Figures S4F–S4H). In
summary, these data conﬁrmed speciﬁc labeling of naive B cell
but not GC B cells. To determine if we could track labeled B cells
seeding a GC reaction, we treated mice with tamoxifen and
immunized them with TM4-Core 2 weeks later (Figure S5A).
2 weeks after immunization, 13%–20% of the naive compart-

S5D). Notably, both labeled and unlabeled GC B cells showed
the same level of TM4-Core binding by ﬂow cytometry (Fig-

treatment in Cd55CreERT2/+ R26tdT/+ mice.
To determine whether B cells enter ongoing GCs, we immu-
nized naive Cd55CreERT2/+R26tdT/+ mice with TM4-Core and
labeled the follicular B cell pool by tamoxifen treatment
11 days after immunization (Figures 4A–4C). Labeling was
evident in the naive B cell compartment as early as 2 days after
tamoxifen administration (Figure 4C). In contrast, small numbers
of tdT+ GC B cells were not detectable until 1 week later
(Figures 4B and 4C). These cells continued to accumulate in
the GC over the ensuing 21-day observation period, reaching a

ongoing GCs.20,23,24 However, whether this occurs in polyclonal
immune responses and how this phenomenon is regulated or
might contribute to GC B cell recruitment has not been exam-
ined. Here, we used two different lineage-tracing approaches
to determine that GC entry by polyclonal B cells is an ongoing
component of the immune response to a complex protein anti-
gen in mice. In the later stages of the GC, this phenomenon
can account for up to 30% or more of the B cells in GCs from
mice immunized with an HIV-1 protein antigen.
B cell entry into GCs is preceded by antigen capture and
migration to the follicular border where activated B cells interact
with Tfh.9,13–15,46 The activated B cell compartment persists
throughout the immune response and continuously produces
memory B cells.33 Our experiments indicate that B cells that
enter this compartment can also invade GCs where they
compete with earlier GC founders. As might be expected from
the observation that AID, the enzyme that mediates somatic mu-
tation, is dependent on cell division for its activity,47 invaders
with shorter GC dwell times are characterized by low levels of so-
matic mutation.48,49
GCs persist for long periods of time in some viral infections or
immunization.50–53 Even acute infections as exempliﬁed by
SARS-CoV-2 can produce long lived GC responses that drive
persisting clonal evolution and accumulation of somatic muta-
tion for up to 1 year after initial infection.54–57 In chronic infec-
tions, such as HIV-1, antibody genes can accumulate exception-
ally high levels of mutations which reﬂect long GC dwell times
and multiple rounds of division and selection and possible GC
re-entry by memory B cells.58 Consistent with the idea that inva-
sion contributes to other immune responses, B cells with only
small numbers of mutations can also be found in prolonged
human GCs formed in response to inﬂuenza vaccine.59
In transgenic mouse experiments, GC invasion was competi-
tive and gated by afﬁnity and precursor frequency.9,17,18 Trans-
genic B cells expressing high afﬁnity receptors for NP transferred
into mice immunized with NP-OVA invaded ongoing GCs.20
Similarly, transgenic B cells with high-afﬁnity lymphocytic cho-
riomeningitis virus (LCMV)-speciﬁc BCRs transferred into mice
chronically infected with LCMV entered ongoing GC re-
sponses.22 In contrast, B cells carrying low afﬁnity receptors
for NP were only able to join in the absence of competi-
tion.9,12,20,60 Similarly, in the relative absence of competition,
transgenic B cells expressing an anti-HIV-1 antibody precursor
with no measurable afﬁnity for the immunizing antigen entered
GCs and developed into broadly neutralizing antibodies.19
If entry is determined by both relative afﬁnity and precursor fre-
quency, why do GC invaders show relatively low afﬁnity for the
immunogen compared with GC founders? GC entry is a compet-
itive process but initial recruitment into the activated B cell
compartment is far less stringent and includes B cells with very
low afﬁnity receptors that cannot be detected by ﬂow cytome-
try.33 B cells in the activated compartment undergo a small
number of divisions and carry a correspondingly low number of
somatic mutations. In the early phases of the immune response,
many of the low afﬁnity cells in the activated B cell compartment
enter the memory compartment where they add to the diversity
to the immune repertoire.61,62 Antigen availability in the GC is
limiting in this early phase of the immune response because it
is not retained and low afﬁnity cells are outcompeted by higher
afﬁnity cells for GC entry. However, in the later stages of the
response, antigen-speciﬁc antibodies are produced, and the re-
sulting antigen-antibody complexes are trapped on follicular
dendritic cells, leading to increased local antigen concentration
and avidity effects that can partially negate differences in afﬁn-
ity.63 The increased local antigen concentration in the form of
multivalent complexes would make it more difﬁcult to distinguish
between B cells expressing low and high afﬁnity receptors. Thus,
although high-afﬁnity antibodies to initially dominant epitopes
block entry and competition by B cells expressing receptors
for their epitopes,41–44 they simultaneously lower the afﬁnity
threshold for entry by B cells speciﬁc for non-dominant or off
target epitopes. In combination, these two effects diversify the
immune response.
In mice where the total number of B cells limits the size of the
naive repertoire to approximately 108 cells, the number of high
afﬁnity precursors for any given antigen is limited. For example,
the number of naive B cells expressing high afﬁnity antibodies to
allophycocyanin in a mouse is estimated at 4,000.64 Should
these relatively rare high afﬁnity cells become partially depleted

in the initial rounds of GC selection, the more abundant B cells
expressing low afﬁnity receptors would become more competi-
tive. Further diversiﬁcation by somatic mutation and selection of
the low afﬁnity cells would expand the repertoire and the diver-
sity the repertoire of antibodies produced to any given pathogen.
Although the number of B cells in humans is orders of magnitude
greater than in the mouse, even the human repertoire is limited
and would similarly beneﬁt from GC diversiﬁcation.
In addition to low afﬁnity invaders, GCs also contain cells with
no measurable afﬁnity for the immunogen.11,62 The latter might
express antibodies that bind to the antigen but with afﬁnities
that are below the level of detection in our assays. Alternatively,
they may express receptors speciﬁc for novel epitopes on the
immunogen uncovered by partial denaturation or degradation
in vivo. However, another possibility is that these B cells are spe-
ciﬁc for completely unrelated environmental or gut antigens and
that they enter the GC to ﬁll a niche vacated by the combination
of ongoing cell death and memory and plasma cell development.
GC invasion by B cells speciﬁc for unrelated antigens has been
documented using transgenic B cells, but only in the presence
of speciﬁc cognate T cell help.23,65 Irrespective of their speci-
ﬁcity, B cells with low or no measurable afﬁnity for the immu-
nogen make up most of the memory B cell compartment and
contribute to its diversity.62
In humans, SARS-CoV-2 infection by Wuhan-hu initially pro-
duces antibodies with relatively limited activity against other var-
iants.66 Ongoing clonal evolution and mutation produces mem-
ory B cells that express antibodies with sufﬁcient breadth to
neutralize large panels of variants.55–57,66 Moreover, there is
persistent emergence of new clones of anti-SARS-CoV-2 B cells
that target an evolving set of epitopes directed in part by pre-ex-
isting antibodies.45,57,67 Thus, both mutation and the emergence
of late appearing clones contribute to the diversiﬁcation of the
anti-SARS-CoV-2 immune response.
This phenomenon may be particularly important in the devel-
opment of broadly neutralizing HIV-1 antibodies where the un-
mutated ancestors of the broadly neutralizing antibodies have lit-
tle or no measurable afﬁnity for the virus and must acquire large
numbers of somatic mutations to develop breadth and po-
tency.58,68 Thus, the B cell precursors of broadly neutralizing an-
tibodies would not be able to enter the GC in the presence of
higher afﬁnity competitors but might be able to join at later
stages of the response. Consistent with this idea, broadly
neutralizing antibody development begins only weeks or months
after initial infection.69–71
In conclusion, GC invasion is an ongoing feature of immune re-
sponses. In mice, this phenomenon helps diversify immunity by
recruiting B cells that express low afﬁnity receptors to GCs
where somatic mutation produces related families of memory
B cells expressing antibodies with varying levels of afﬁnity for
the immunogen. This mechanism may be particularly valuable
when the system is faced with diversifying pathogens such as
SARS-CoV-2 or HIV-1.
Limitations of the study
The mouse models and immunizations performed in our experi-
ments are useful for fate-mapping resident and invader GC
B cells in the draining lymph node. However, they do not allow
us to follow these populations over time because the fate map-
ped cells cannot be distinguished from immunization indepen-
dent GCs formed in parallel in the gut-, skin-, or nasal-associated
lymphoid tissues. The same challenge limits our ability to deter-
mine the contribution of resident and invader populations to the
overall plasma cells and memory B cell compartments. We
determined that passively transferred antibodies before immuni-
zation lowers the afﬁnity threshold for GC B cell recruitment.
However, our mouse model does not allow us to interrogate
the role of antibody feedback in late entry GC cells as it would
require that we untangle the role of the initial endogenous versus
the passively transferred antibody.

RNA-seq
Cells were sorted directly into a solution of 1 % 2-mercaptoethanol (Sigma) in TCL Buffer (1031576; QIAGEN), and RNA was isolated
using RNAClean XP Beads (A63987; Beckman Coulter). Near full-length mRNA was reverse transcribed as previously described.76,77
1 ng of total RNA was used to generate full length cDNA using the SMART-Seq v4 Ultra Low Input RNA Kit (Clontech, Cat # 634888).
1 ng of cDNA was then used to prepare libraries using Illumina Nextera XT DNA sample preparation kit (Cat# FC-131-1024). Libraries
with unique barcodes were pooled at equal molar ratios and sequenced on Illumina NextSeq 500 sequencer to generate 75 bp single
reads, following manufacturer’s protocol (Cat# 15048776 Rev.E).
Sequence reads were pseudo-aligned to an index created from the Ensembl mouse GRCm38.p5 assembly. Transcript-level abun-
dances were quantiﬁed using kallisto v0.44.078 and subsequently summarized to gene level using the R package tximport.79 Counts
normalization and differential gene expression analysis were performed using DESeq2.80 Data ﬁles for the RNA-seq analyses have
been deposited in the National Center for Biotechnology Information Gene Expression Omnibus under accession no. GSE218272.
Single-cell index sorting and RNA puriﬁcation
Activated B cells and GC cells from popliteal lymph node were stained, single cell sorted with a FACS Aria II (BD) in 96-well plates
containing 5 mL lysis buffer (TCL buffer (Qiagen, 1031576) 1 % 2-mercaptoethanol (Sigma, M3148) and immediately frozen at -80C.
Single-cell RNA was puriﬁed using magnetic beads (RNAClean XP, Beckman Coulter, A63987) following the manufacturer’s in-
structions. RNA was eluted from the magnetic beads with 11 mL of a solution containing: random primers (14.5 ng/mL Invitrogen,
48190-011), tergitol (0.5 % of NP-40 70 % in H2O, Sigma-Aldrich, NP40S) and RNase inhibitor (0.6 U/mL, Promega, N2615) in
nuclease-free water (Qiagen), and incubated at 65C for 3 min. cDNA was subsequently synthesized by reverse transcription with
7 mL of a solution containing: SuperScript III Reverse Transcriptase, 5x buffer, DTT (SuperScript III Reverse Transcriptase, Invitrogen,
18080-044, 10,000U) dNTP (25 mM) RNase inhibitor (0.6 U/mL, Promega, N2615) in nuclease-free water (Qiagen) incubated at 42 C
10 min, 25 C 10 min, 50 C 60 min, 94 C 5 min). cDNA was stored at -20 C or immediately used for antibody gene ampliﬁcation by
nested polymerase chain reaction (PCR) after addition of 10 mL of nuclease-free water.

Fab afﬁnity and avidity analyses
Biolayer interferometry assays were performed on the Octet Red instrument (ForteBio) at 30 C with shaking at 1,000 rpm.
Afﬁnity measurement
All measurements of HIV-1 TM4-Core/Fabs binding were corrected by subtracting the signal obtained from traces performed with
TM4-Core but in absence of Fabs. The same trimer used for the immunization was biotinylated using EZ-Link NHS-PEG4-Biotin
at a ratio of one biotin molecule per trimer and un-ligated biotin was removed using Zebra desalting columns (Thermo Scientiﬁc,
21330). The kinetic analysis using high precision streptavidin biosensor (ForteBio 18-5118) was performed as follows: (1) baseline:
60 s immersion in buffer (kinetics buffer 10x ForteBio 18-1105), (2) loading: 200 s immersion in a solution with Biotinylated trimeric
TM4-core at 400 nM, (3) baseline: 200 s immersion in buffer, (4) Association: 300 s immersion in solution with Fab 10 mg/mL, (5) disso-
ciation: 600 s immersion in buffer. Curve ﬁtting was performed using a fast 2:1 binding model and the Data analysis software
(ForteBio).
Avidity measurement
All measurements of Fab/TM4-Core binding were corrected by subtracting the signal obtained from traces performed with the same
Fab but in absence of TM4-Core. TM4-Core was used as the same trimer used for the immunization or as a tetrameric TM4-Core
protein after incubation of the biotinylated trimer with streptavidin. The kinetic analysis using FAB2G biosensor (ForteBio 18-5125)
was performed as for the afﬁnity measurement. Curve ﬁtting was performed using a bivalent model and the data analysis software
(ForteBio). The same protocol was used with multimerized TM4-Core.
Multiphoton imaging
pLNs were collected and imaged as previously described.10 In brief, adipose tissue and excess epithelium were removed under a
dissecting microscope, and pLNs were mounted in phosphate-buffered saline (PBS) between two coverslips held together with vac-
uum grease.82 Mounted pLNs were imaged on an Olympus FV1000 upright microscope with a 325 Plan water-immersion objective
(numerical aperture (NA) 1.05) and a Mai-Tai DeepSee titanium-sapphire laser (Spectraphysics). tdTomato and GFP were imaged at
an excitation wavelength of l = 930 nm. Fluorescence emission was collected in two channels, with a 525/50 nm ﬁlter to detect GFP,
and 605/70 nm ﬁlter to detect RFP.
pLNs were embedded in 4 % low-melt agarose NuSieve GTG (Lonza), previously heated to boiling and then cooled to 37C before
embedding and cut into 200 mm slices using a Leica VT1000A vibratome. Slices were then held between coverslips for imaging.
Imaged LN slices were cut into two or more fragments using a #10 disposable scalpel (Miltex) under a Leica MZFLIII ﬂuorescence
stereomicroscope ﬁtted with a PlanAPO 1.0 X objective. LN slices were placed in microcentrifuge tubes containing 100 ml of PBS
supplemented with 0.5 % BSA and 1 mM EDTA (Invitrogen), macerated using disposable micropestles (Axygen), and further disso-
ciated into single-cell suspensions by gentle vortexing. 100 ml of 2X antibody stain (antibodies to B220, CD38, IgD, CD95, GL7, CD8a,
CD4, NK1.1, GR-1, F4/80 supplemented with Fc block) was added to the cell suspension and incubated on ice for 30 min. Single cells
were sorted into 96-well plates, as described above, using a FACS Aria II cell sorter.
Olympus Imaging Files generated while scanning multiple ﬁelds of view were automatically stitched together to generate compiled
views of entire lymph node slices using the FluoView FV1000 software accompanying the FV1000 microscope.
